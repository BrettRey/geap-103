# GEAP 103 Assessment Framework (Draft v6)

**Course:** GEAP 103 Basic Computer Skills
**Version:** Draft v6, 2026-03-25

**Changes from v5:** (1) Semester timeline staggered — DLs close Week 11, Git Episodes close Week 12, Week 14 = 10% only. (2) Micro-Defence 3 moved to Week 11. (3) Evidence-Indexed Reflection changed from written to oral one-on-one with instructor, Week 13 (4 min + pre-tagging). (4) Micro-defence format specified as simultaneous triads with observation tracking. (5) Task criteria evolve across Weeks 4, 8, 11 (3 → 5 → 7 criteria). (6) Weekly speaking practice (session review) documented as formative thread feeding micro-defences and oral reflection. (7) Within-component weighting for micro-defences specified (equal). (8) Session review scenarios specified for all 14 weeks.

- Percentage grades (Humber standard)

## Assessment Details

### 1. AI Decision Logs (30%)

**What:** Structured documentation of AI-assisted work, curated by student, with self-assessment.

**Format:** Template-based log entries (max 8 across semester, student-selected as best evidence):

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| Task goal + constraints | 1–2 sentences |
| AI used | Which AI tool and context (e.g., Copilot in Word, Claude, a coding agent, etc.) |
| Prompt snippet(s) | Max 3 turns (copy/paste or screenshot) |
| Output summary | What the AI proposed (1–2 sentences) |
| Decision | Accept / Modify / Reject |
| Verification | How they checked (ran it, used Accessibility Checker, tested on sample, etc.) |
| What changed | 1–2 bullets |
| **Self-assessment** | **Student rates own entry against rubric (Excellent / Proficient / Developing / Insufficient) + 1 sentence justifying the rating** |

**Evidence cap:** ~150–200 words student-authored text per entry (excluding pasted AI excerpt and self-assessment sentence).

**Entry window:** Weeks 2–11. Students may draw entries from any week in this window. With 10 weeks of potential entries and a requirement of 8 curated ones, there is ample selection. The window closes at Week 11 so the instructor can read and score the DLs before conducting oral reflections in Week 13.

**Failure/rejection episodes:** Entries documenting cases where AI output was wrong or misleading are encouraged throughout. At least one such entry is expected across the 8 submissions, but all entries that document critical evaluation (including rejection or substantial revision of AI output) are valued. Quality of the evaluation matters more than the number of failure episodes.

**CLO Alignment:**
- CLO 3: Request formulation, prompt revision
- CLO 4: Evaluation of AI outputs, decision documentation

**Grading Criteria:**

| Level | Description | % Range |
|-------|-------------|---------|
| Excellent | Clear and progressively ambitious goals across entries; systematic evaluation; substantive decisions with rationale; evidence of both exploration (trying new approaches) and exploitation (going deeper on what works); failure/rejection episodes present; self-assessments are well calibrated | 85–100 |
| Proficient | Goals stated with some ambition; evaluation present; decisions documented; entries show range of tasks attempted (not just 8 entries about the same trivial task); at least one failure/rejection episode; self-assessments present | 70–84 |
| Developing | Goals vague or consistently trivial; evaluation superficial; minimal decision rationale; little evidence of exploration or growth in ambition; self-assessments missing or poorly calibrated | 50–69 |
| Insufficient | Missing elements, no critical evaluation visible, no self-assessment | 0–49 |

*Note on ambition: "Ambitious" is calibrated to B1 — it means the student attempted something that genuinely challenged their ability to articulate what they wanted, not something technically sophisticated. A student who struggles to describe a complex goal and documents that struggle is demonstrating more than a student who describes a trivial goal perfectly. The language is B1; the ambition can be high.*

**Feedback cycle:** Instructor provides written feedback on entries 1–2 (submitted by ~Week 4) before entry 3 is due. Feedback highlights one strength and one specific improvement target. Students are expected to identify in a subsequent entry what they changed based on feedback. A second feedback round covers entries 3–5 (by ~Week 8). Entries 6–8 are assessed summatively. The DL self-assessments also inform the micro-defence check-ins: patterns of over- or under-rating are discussed in the one-on-one moments during triads.

### 2. Oral Micro-Defences + Written Artifacts (25%)

**What:** Three check-ins across the semester combining written technical communication, oral demonstration of understanding, and peer feedback.

**Schedule:** Weeks 4, 8, 11

**Format: Simultaneous triads.** All students present at the same time in groups of 3. Each student presents to two partners, who give structured feedback. The instructor circulates with scoring sheets, sampling each group and asking one follow-up question per presenter. All students assessed in one ~30-minute block with no overflow into the following week.

**Process:**
1. **In-class written diagnostic** (10 min, unmediated, whole class simultaneous): Brief writing task completed in class without AI assistance. Handwritten or on a locked-down device.
2. **Setup + triad formation** (~5–7 min): Students move into pre-assigned groups of 3, open their artifact.
3. **Three rotations** (~9 min each): Each student presents for 4 min, then receives feedback from both partners (~5 min). Instructor circulates, spending 2–3 min per group, asks one follow-up question, scores.
4. Student submits 2 written artifacts before each check-in (e.g., help request + troubleshooting log, or GitHub issue + collaboration note). These may be AI-assisted.

**Make-up for absent students:** Students who miss a micro-defence week write the diagnostic during settling-in the following week, then do an ad hoc triad (instructor + 2 volunteers) during any independent work period. The instructor hears the full presentation.

**Written artifact types (CLO 5):** These are genres of technical communication, not tied to specific tools:
- Help request
- Troubleshooting log
- Bug report
- Issue description (e.g., GitHub Issue)
- Change description (e.g., PR description)
- Collaboration note / handoff documentation
- Process documentation from an artifact package

**Scoring (per check-in):**

**Oral fluency component (30%):**

| Band | Description | % Range |
|------|-------------|---------|
| Above target | Relative ease, able to keep going, pauses for planning evident but doesn't lose thread | 75–84 |
| Target | Can keep going in a way people understand, pauses regularly to think | 65–74 |
| Approaching target | Can say longer things but slow, sometimes has to give up | 50–64 |
| Below threshold | Very short contributions, mostly words/phrases | 0–49 |

*Note: These bands describe oral performance on course tasks. They are not CEFR proficiency ratings. See "B1.2 Exit Evidence" section for how oral performance contributes to the exit proficiency judgment.*

**Task component (50%) — evolving checklist:**

The task criteria expand across the three check-ins, reflecting the growing range of skills students are expected to demonstrate:

**Week 4 (diagnostic baseline — 3 criteria):**

| Criterion | Evidence |
|-----------|----------|
| Clear description of what was done + context | ✓ / partial / ✗ |
| Correct use of essential terminology | ✓ / partial / ✗ |
| Explains a decision or choice (why, not just what) | ✓ / partial / ✗ |

**Week 8 (formative — 5 criteria, adding CLO 2):**

| Criterion | Evidence |
|-----------|----------|
| Clear description of what was done + context | ✓ / partial / ✗ |
| Correct use of essential terminology | ✓ / partial / ✗ |
| Explains a decision or choice (why, not just what) | ✓ / partial / ✗ |
| Shows what was tried (not just "it doesn't work") | ✓ / partial / ✗ |
| Interprets instruction/error message accurately | ✓ / partial / ✗ |

**Week 11 (summative — 7 criteria, adding CLOs 3–4):**

| Criterion | Evidence |
|-----------|----------|
| Clear description of what was done + context | ✓ / partial / ✗ |
| Correct use of essential terminology | ✓ / partial / ✗ |
| Explains a decision or choice (why, not just what) | ✓ / partial / ✗ |
| Shows what was tried (not just "it doesn't work") | ✓ / partial / ✗ |
| Interprets instruction/error message accurately | ✓ / partial / ✗ |
| Describes how AI was directed (what the student asked for and why) | ✓ / partial / ✗ |
| Evaluates AI output (what was accepted, modified, or rejected and why) | ✓ / partial / ✗ |

*Rationale for evolution: Week 4 is a diagnostic baseline — students are explaining work for the first time. By Week 8, CLO 2 (troubleshooting) is active and students should be able to narrate problem-solving. By Week 11, CLOs 3–4 (AI direction and evaluation) should be visible in oral explanation. The first three criteria are constant, providing longitudinal comparability; the additional criteria capture growing competence.*

**In-class written diagnostic (10%):** Assessed holistically on communicative effectiveness. This component provides unmediated written evidence of the student's independent language use.

**Peer feedback (10%):** Assessed on whether the peer's feedback is specific, uses course terminology, and identifies a genuine strength or question. (The purpose is to develop the peer's evaluative judgment, not to grade the presenter.) In the triad format, each presenter receives feedback from two peers.

**CLO Alignment:**
- CLO 2: Interpret technical instructions, troubleshoot, justify solutions (from Week 8)
- CLO 5: Communicate technical information clearly in professional formats
- CLOs 3, 4: Direct and evaluate AI (from Week 11)

**Time requirement:** Diagnostic is whole-class simultaneous (10 min). Oral defences use simultaneous triads (groups of 3, all presenting at once): ~28–30 min per round for any class size. Instructor circulates and samples each group. Total in-class time per round: ~50 min (diagnostic + setup + triads).

**Instructor sampling:** With ~7 groups and 28 min across 3 rounds, the instructor will hear ~15 of ~21 presenters substantively (2–3 min each). The remaining students are scored on briefer observation. The scoring sheet includes an "evidence basis" field (full observation / partial observation / deferred) for transparency.

**Observation tracking protocol:** After each micro-defence round, the instructor records which students received "partial" or "deferred" observation. At the next round, those students' groups are visited first. This guarantees every student receives at least 2 full observations across the 3 rounds. A simple rotation checklist (student name × 3 rounds, mark F/P/D) is printed alongside the scoring sheets. If a student has been partially observed at both Weeks 4 and 8, their group is the instructor's first stop at Week 11.

**Within-component weighting:** The three micro-defence rounds are weighted equally (~8.3% each within the 25% allocation). Equal weighting is appropriate because the evolving criteria already reflect rising expectations: meeting 3/3 at Week 4 represents diagnostic-level performance, while meeting 7/7 at Week 11 represents summative-level performance. The scores are comparable in meaning (proportion of applicable criteria met), even though the criteria themselves expand.

### 3. Artifact Packages (25%)

**What:** Three artifact packages of increasing complexity. Each package includes the product itself AND the documentation, explanation, and sharing that surround it. The artifact is the communicative package, not just the thing.

**An artifact package includes:**
- The product (a digital artifact: document, tool, visualisation, application, or whatever the student built)
- Process documentation in the student's own words: what AI did, what the student changed and why, what was checked
- Evidence of sharing or explaining: how the student presented, described, or taught the work to someone else

**Submissions:**

| Package | Week | Weight | Requirements |
|---------|------|--------|--------------|
| Organised file system | 4 | 7% | Logical folder structure, consistent naming, version awareness |
| Mid-semester artifact | 10 | 8% | A working artifact addressing a genuine need, with accessibility where applicable, accompanied by process documentation and evidence of sharing |
| Final artifact | 14 | 10% | More complex or ambitious artifact, iteration visible, documentation shows how the work was explained to others |

*The artifact types are not constrained to Office 365 outputs. Students may build documents, spreadsheets, presentations, visualisations, apps, interactive tools, data pipelines, or whatever serves their goal. The assessment is on the quality of the communicative package: can the student articulate what they built, why, how, and what they learned?*

**Process documentation (required for each package):** Written in the student's own words (not AI-generated):
- What AI did and what the student directed
- What the student changed and why
- What the student checked or verified
- What was shared with others and how (peer review, presentation, explanation)

*The process documentation is a window into the student's independent language use and understanding. Generic or clearly AI-generated notes (e.g., fluent prose markedly above the student's demonstrated oral level) will be flagged for oral follow-up.*

**Accessibility:** Where the artifact is a document, presentation, or other content intended for an audience, accessibility features (headings, alt text, accessible colour, etc.) are expected. The specific tools used to check accessibility may vary; the principle is that the student considered their audience.

**CLO Alignment:**
- CLO 1: Manage files and versions (file organisation, naming, version control)
- CLO 4: Evaluate AI outputs (via process documentation)
- CLO 6: Produce digital products to college standards

**Grading Criteria:**

| Level | Description | % Range |
|-------|-------------|---------|
| Excellent | Ambitious attempt; product is functional and accessible; process documentation shows thoughtful AI collaboration in student's own language; evidence of sharing/explaining the work to others; student can articulate what they built, why, and what they learned | 85–100 |
| Proficient | Substantive attempt; product is functional and mostly accessible; process documentation present and in own words; some evidence of sharing | 70–84 |
| Developing | Safe or trivial attempt; product functional with issues; process documentation incomplete or possibly AI-generated; limited evidence of sharing | 50–69 |
| Insufficient | Minimal attempt; non-functional product; missing process documentation | 0–49 |

*Note on ambition: "Ambitious" and "substantive" are calibrated to B1. A student who attempts something that stretches their ability to describe it is being ambitious. A student who sticks to what they can already articulate easily is playing it safe. The framework values the stretch.*

### 4. Git Episodes (10%)

**What:** Evidence of specific Git/version control competencies through required episodes, not quantity metrics.

**Required episodes (each demonstrated once, Weeks 6–12):**

| Episode | Evidence Required | CLO |
|---------|-------------------|-----|
| **Restore** | Demonstrate recovering an earlier version (screenshot/log of revert/checkout/restore) | 1 |
| **Error recovery** | One "something broke → diagnosis → fix" sequence with error text captured | 1, 2 |
| **Collaboration** | One PR (or equivalent) with description + one comment exchange | 7 |

**Submission deadline:** Week 12. By this point, students have had 7 weeks of Git activity. The collaboration episode arises naturally from the Phase 3 group work in Weeks 11–12. Closing Git Episodes in Week 12 (alongside Decision Logs) clears Week 13 for the oral reflection.

*Conflict Resolution was included in v2 but removed after review. Merge conflict resolution requires a conceptual model of branching and state that is unrealistic for this population in 14 weeks. If authentic conflicts arise during collaborative work, they can be documented as bonus evidence, but they are not required.*

**Assessment:** Checklist-based. Short oral confirmation for any suspiciously "perfect" artifact.

**Target mental model:** By Week 14, students should understand version history as a timeline of saved states that can be navigated (not just "undo"). They should be able to explain, in simple terms, that a commit is a snapshot, that restoring means going back to an earlier snapshot, and that collaboration means two people contributing snapshots to the same timeline. The command-line details are secondary to this conceptual understanding.

**CLO Alignment:**
- CLO 1: Manage files and versions using version-control tools
- CLO 7: Collaborate using shared practices

**Grading:**

| Level | Description | % Range |
|-------|-------------|---------|
| Complete | All 3 episodes documented with clear evidence | 85–100 |
| Mostly complete | 2 episodes documented clearly, 1 weak or missing | 70–84 |
| Partial | 1 episode documented clearly | 50–69 |
| Insufficient | No episodes with clear evidence | 0–49 |

### 5. Oral Evidence-Indexed Reflection (10%)

**What:** A one-on-one conversation between the student and the instructor, in which the student makes claims about their learning, points to specific evidence, and evaluates how strong that evidence is.

**Format:** Individual oral reflection (~4 min per student + 1 min transition), conducted during Week 13. The student has their portfolio open with evidence pre-tagged (see below). The instructor selects 2 CLOs to discuss (not all 7 — the oral format samples rather than exhausts).

**Pre-tagging (5 min, whole class, start of Week 13):** Before reflections begin, students spend 5 minutes tagging their portfolio: for 3–4 CLOs, they open or bookmark the specific artifact they would point to as evidence. This reduces navigation friction during the conversation and ensures the 4 minutes are spent on articulation and evaluation, not searching for files.

**Process:**
1. Instructor asks: "Pick one CLO you feel strongest about. Show me the evidence."
2. Student makes a claim ("I can now..."), points to a pre-tagged artifact, and evaluates the strength of that evidence ("This is strong evidence because..." or "This is OK but I wish I had...").
3. Instructor asks one follow-up question, then selects a second CLO (typically one the student might be weaker on).
4. Student responds. Hard stop at 4 minutes — use a visible timer.

**This is unmediated.** No AI assistance. The student's portfolio is open for reference (they need to find and point to evidence), but the language is entirely their own. This is the culminating unmediated spoken performance of the semester.

**Why oral, not written:** The oral format produces stronger validity evidence than the written alternative. At B1, students can articulate reflective claims more naturally in speech than in writing. The one-on-one format allows the instructor to probe understanding ("Can you tell me more about why you chose that example?") in a way that written submissions cannot. It also eliminates authenticity concerns — the student is speaking in real time, with their portfolio visible.

**Why one-on-one, not triads:** Reflection on one's own learning is personal. A student cannot usefully serve as audience for another student's CLO-indexed self-assessment. The triad format works for technical explanations (Weeks 4, 8, 11) because the audience can evaluate clarity. It does not work for metacognitive reflection because there is no information gap — the peers have no basis for feedback.

**Connection to session review speaking practice:** Students have been practising explaining their work to AI every week since Week 1. The session review is low-stakes rehearsal for this culminating conversation. By Week 13, explaining work orally is a well-practised skill, not a novel demand.

**Logistics:** At 5 min per student (4 min conversation + 1 min transition):

| Class size | Reflection time | Remaining for structured work |
|-----------|----------------|-------------------------------|
| 15 | 75 min | 70 min |
| 20 | 100 min | 45 min |
| 24 | 120 min | 25 min |

For classes above 20, the instructor may begin reflections during late Segment B (before break) and continue through Segment C. For classes of 24, the reflections consume most of the session; the structured work task (see below) must be self-directing.

**Structured work during reflections:** Students not currently in a reflection work on a finalization checklist:

1. Run Accessibility Checker on your final artifact. Fix any issues.
2. Complete your process documentation (what AI did, what you changed, what you checked).
3. Review your peer response notes — have you incorporated the feedback?
4. Prepare your sharing/presentation for Week 14 (1 min explanation of one design decision).

This checklist is a deliverable: students submit it (checked off, with notes) by end of session. It is ungraded but provides structure and accountability during the 100–120 minutes when the instructor is conducting reflections.

**CLO Alignment:** All (metacognitive synthesis and evaluative judgment)

**Grading Criteria:**

| Level | Description | % Range |
|-------|-------------|---------|
| Excellent | Specific claims about 2 CLOs; points to appropriate evidence (including Decision Logs, artifacts, Git history, Goal Document, and/or session review transcripts); honest evaluation of evidence strength ("this is strong because..." / "this is weaker because..."); genuine identification of areas for growth; can answer follow-up questions | 85–100 |
| Proficient | Claims present for 2–3 CLOs; evidence identified; some evaluation of evidence quality; follow-up answers adequate | 70–84 |
| Developing | General claims; vague evidence links ("I did stuff with Git"); no evaluation of evidence quality; struggles with follow-up questions | 50–69 |
| Insufficient | Cannot articulate claims; no evidence identified; cannot engage with follow-up | 0–49 |

## Weekly Speaking Practice (Session Review)

**What:** A 10–15 minute AI-assisted speaking practice at the end of each class (Weeks 1–14). Students talk to AI using voice mode, practising the same oral skills assessed in micro-defences and the oral reflection.

**Setup:** Students say or paste a role-setting prompt at the start: "Speaking practice. I'm a B1 English student practising explaining my work. Ask me follow-up questions. If my explanation is vague, tell me what's unclear and ask me to be more specific. Don't do the explaining for me." This prompt is on the weekly slide and in the `copilot-instructions.md` file in the starter zip.

**Weekly scenarios** (each matched to that week's learning):

| Week | Scenario | AI role | Communicative demand |
|------|----------|---------|---------------------|
| 1 | Tell AI what you set up today and what you want to learn. | Curious classmate | Expressing desires, answering follow-ups |
| 2 | Describe what happened when you tried your prompt — what you expected vs. what you got. | Specificity coach | Evaluating results, being precise about gaps |
| 3 | Explain what a commit message is and why yours was better than AI's suggestion. | Confused classmate who doesn't understand Git | Explaining a technical decision to a non-expert |
| 4 | No session review (diagnostic + triad defence are the reflection activities). | — | — |
| 5 | Describe an accessibility problem you found, what the Checker said, and how you fixed it. | Help desk | Narrating a troubleshooting sequence |
| 6 | You made a change to a shared document. Explain what you changed and why to someone who needs to review it. | Teammate reviewing your work | Justifying a change to someone with authority to reject it |
| 7 | A collaborator's PR has a problem. Describe the problem clearly enough for them to fix it without your help. | Collaborator who can't see your screen | Writing/speaking a bug report with reproduction steps |
| 8 | No session review (diagnostic + triad defence are the reflection activities). | — | — |
| 9 | You built something but it doesn't do what you intended. Explain to AI what you wanted, what you got, and what you think went wrong. | Debugging partner | Diagnosing a gap between intention and result |
| 10 | Present your mid-semester artifact to AI as if it were a client. Explain what it does, who it's for, and one decision you made during the process. | Client who commissioned the work | Presenting work to a non-technical audience |
| 11 | No session review (diagnostic + triad defence are the reflection activities). | — | — |
| 12 | AI plays an instructor asking about one of your Decision Log entries. Defend your self-assessment rating. | Sceptical instructor | Justifying a self-evaluation with evidence |
| 13 | No session review (oral reflection is the reflection activity). | — | — |
| 14 | Explain your final artifact to AI as if AI were a new student who will take this course next semester. What should they know? | Future student | Teaching/transferring knowledge to a newcomer |

**Design note on AI role-setting:** The role-setting prompt ("Speaking practice. I'm a B1 English student...") deliberately instructs AI to push back when explanations are vague but does not instruct AI to model vocabulary. This is intentional: the goal is to force production, not supply language. If the student says "the thing broke," AI should ask "What thing? What happened?" rather than offering "Do you mean you got an error message?" Instructors should understand this design choice and not modify the prompt to add vocabulary modelling, which would undermine the production demand.

**Assessment:** Not graded. The session review is formative — it builds the speaking skills assessed in micro-defences (Weeks 4, 8, 11) and the oral reflection (Week 13). Students may save transcripts as portfolio evidence for Decision Logs or the oral reflection.

**Optional phone recording:** Students may record their session review using a phone on a lanyard (chest-level, ~20 cm from mouth). The recording is for self-review ("Listen to yourself. Did you say what you meant?"), not graded. Introduced in Week 4 alongside the triad defence.

## Feedback Architecture

Ongoing assessments function as learning progressions only if feedback is timely, specific, and acted upon.

### Decision Logs
- **Round 1** (entries 1–2): Instructor feedback returned by Week 6. One strength, one specific improvement target.
- **Round 2** (entries 3–5): Instructor feedback returned by Week 10. Focus on whether Round 1 feedback was incorporated.
- **Entries 6–8:** Assessed summatively (submitted Week 11).
- **Self-assessment calibration:** Instructor notes where student self-ratings diverge significantly from instructor assessment. Patterns of over- or under-rating are discussed during the micro-defence circulation (brief one-on-one moment during triads).

### Oral Micro-Defences
- **Peer feedback** at each check-in gives the presenter immediate, structured input from two classmates (triad format).
- **Instructor feedback** is oral and immediate (during circulation). Written scores are returned within one week.
- **Progression signal:** The first three task criteria are constant across all three check-ins; additional criteria appear at Weeks 8 and 11. Students can track their own improvement on the core criteria while seeing new demands emerge.

### Artifact Packages
- **Week 4 package** receives written feedback by Week 6, explicitly identifying what to carry forward (in process documentation quality and ambition, not just product quality).
- **Week 10 package** receives written feedback by Week 12, with attention to whether the student's ambition has grown and whether the communicative package (documentation, sharing) is developing.
- **Week 14 package** is assessed summatively.

### Grading Timeline for Instructor
| What | Submitted | Mark by | Notes |
|------|-----------|---------|-------|
| Micro-Defence 3 scoring sheets | Week 11 | Week 12 | Score during triads |
| Decision Logs (8 entries) | Week 11 | Week 12–13 | Read before oral reflections — you need to know each student's portfolio |
| Git Episodes | Week 12 | Week 13 | Checklist-based, ~2 hours |
| Oral Reflection scoring | Week 13 | Week 14 | Score during conversations |
| Final Artifact Package | Week 14 | Exam period | |

## Assessor Guidance

### CLO 2 vs CLO 5 Distinction

For marking reliability:

- **CLO 2** (Troubleshoot): Evidence centres on interpretation and choice under uncertainty. Look for: reading system output, diagnosing problems, selecting a fix, justifying the choice, or describing the problem clearly enough to get help.

- **CLO 5** (Communicate): Evidence centres on packaging information for another person. Look for: genre moves (context, reproduction steps, expected vs. actual), polite tone, concise terminology, would-this-work-if-sent-to-someone test.

A single artifact can support both. Don't collapse them into "they explained something."

### Fluency Rubric Usage

Use fluency rubric only for delivery/flow slice of oral evidence. The "target" band represents the expected performance level for B1.2; "above target" is above expectation for this level. Do not report fluency bands as CEFR levels to students or in institutional records. See "B1.2 Exit Evidence" below.

### Failure Episode Verification

Entries documenting failure/rejection of AI output should show:
1. What the AI got wrong
2. How the student noticed
3. What they did instead

Generic or vague entries ("the AI made a mistake so I fixed it") should be flagged for revision at the next feedback round. The goal is authentic critical evaluation, not a compliance checkbox.

### Process Documentation Authenticity

Compare the language in process documentation against the student's demonstrated oral level at micro-defences. A marked mismatch (e.g., fluent academic prose from a student who struggles to sustain oral turns) warrants a brief oral follow-up: "Can you tell me in your own words what the AI did here?"

### Peer Feedback Quality

Peer feedback is assessed on the *peer's* ability to apply criteria, not on the accuracy of their judgment. Look for: specific reference to the checklist, use of course terminology, a genuine (not generic) observation. "Good job" is insufficient; "You explained what you tried before asking for help, which made the problem clear" is sufficient.

### Oral Reflection Conduct

The oral reflection is a conversation, not an interrogation. The instructor's goal is to elicit the student's genuine self-assessment, not to catch them out. Start with their strongest CLO (let them choose), then move to one you select. The student should feel like they are showing you what they learned, not defending against criticism. If a student freezes, offer a prompt: "Look at your Decision Logs. Which entry are you most proud of? Tell me about it."

## B1.2 Exit Evidence

The B1.2 exit target is a program-level claim supported by converging evidence across the portfolio, not by any single assessment. The evidence base includes:

| Evidence source | Type | What it shows |
|-----------------|------|---------------|
| Oral micro-defences (×3) | Unmediated spoken | Sustained oral communication about technical topics; progression visible across three check-ins |
| In-class written diagnostics (×3) | Unmediated written | Independent written communication without AI assistance |
| Oral reflection (×1) | Unmediated spoken | Metacognitive claims about own learning, indexed to evidence |
| Process documentation (×3) | Semi-independent written | Student's own language describing AI collaboration decisions and explaining/sharing their work |
| Decision Log self-assessments (×8) | Semi-independent written | Student's own evaluative language applied to their work |
| Peer feedback (×3) | Semi-independent written | Student's own evaluative language applied to a peer's work |
| Written artifacts (×6) | AI-assisted | Genre competence in technical communication (with AI support) |
| Session review transcripts (ongoing) | Semi-independent spoken | Sustained practice of oral explanation across 14 weeks |

**What the framework does not claim:** That any single percentage score on any single assessment corresponds to a CEFR level. The fluency rubric bands are course-internal performance descriptors, not CEFR ratings.

**What the framework does claim:** That a student who passes the course (50%+) has demonstrated, through multiple unmediated and semi-independent performances across 14 weeks, communicative competence consistent with B1.2 in the domain of technical/digital communication. The strongest evidence comes from the unmediated sources (oral defences, oral reflection, and in-class diagnostics); the AI-assisted sources provide supporting evidence of genre competence and task articulation.

**Limitation:** This framework assesses language use in a specific domain (digital/technical communication). It does not constitute a general English proficiency assessment. The B1.2 exit target applies to this domain; students' general English proficiency may differ.

## Notes

- Assessment weights: Process (30%) + Language (25%) + Artifact Package (25%) + Collaboration (10%) + Metacognition (10%)
- Early assessments (Weeks 2–6) pitched at A2+; later assessments scaffold toward B1.2
- Git introduced Week 3, assessed from Week 6 (3 weeks practice buffer)
- Oral micro-defences and in-class diagnostics provide unmediated language evidence; written artifacts and decision logs are expected to be AI-assisted (with process documentation and self-assessments in student's own words)
- Accessibility checking tools (e.g., Office 365 Accessibility Checker) treated as authentic workplace tools; specific tools may evolve
- Self- and peer assessment are integrated into existing components (not standalone assessments) to develop evaluative judgment without adding separate graded tasks
- The framework is designed to be tool-independent. AI capabilities will change between the time this framework is written and the course launch. The skills assessed (articulation, evaluation, documentation, communication, collaboration, metacognition) are durable regardless of which tools students use
- Ambition is calibrated to B1: the student attempted something that genuinely challenged their ability to articulate what they wanted. The language level is B1; the project ambition can be high
- Weekly speaking practice (session review) is the formative thread that makes the summative oral assessments work. Without it, the micro-defences and oral reflection would be isolated performances. With it, they are the culmination of a 14-week practice arc.
