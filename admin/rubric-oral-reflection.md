# GEAP 103 -- Oral Evidence-Indexed Reflection Rubric (10%)

**Course:** GEAP 103 Basic Computer Skills
**Assessment:** Oral Evidence-Indexed Reflection
**Weight:** 10% of final grade
**Primary CLOs:** All (metacognitive synthesis and evaluative judgment)

---

## Overview

A one-on-one conversation between the student and the instructor, conducted during Week 13. The student makes claims about their learning, points to specific evidence, and evaluates how strong that evidence is. ~4 min per student + 1 min transition. The instructor selects 2 CLOs to discuss (not all 7 -- the oral format samples rather than exhausts).

This is the culminating unmediated spoken performance of the semester. No AI assistance. The student's portfolio is open for reference (they need to find and point to evidence), but the language is entirely their own.

---

## Process

### Pre-Tagging (5 min, whole class, start of Week 13)

Before reflections begin, students spend 5 minutes tagging their portfolio: for 3--4 CLOs, they open or bookmark the specific artifact they would point to as evidence. This reduces navigation friction during the conversation and ensures the 4 minutes are spent on articulation and evaluation, not searching for files.

### Conversation Structure (~4 min per student)

1. Instructor asks: "Pick one CLO you feel strongest about. Show me the evidence."
2. Student makes a claim ("I can now..."), points to a pre-tagged artifact, and evaluates the strength of that evidence ("This is strong evidence because..." or "This is OK but I wish I had...").
3. Instructor asks one follow-up question, then selects a second CLO (typically one the student might be weaker on).
4. Student responds. Hard stop at 4 minutes -- use a visible timer.

### "Freeze" Prompt

If a student freezes, offer: **"Which DL entry are you most proud of? Tell me about it."**

This is a conversation, not an interrogation. The instructor's goal is to elicit the student's genuine self-assessment, not to catch them out. The student should feel like they are showing you what they learned, not defending against criticism.

---

## Grading Criteria

| Level | Description | % Range |
|-------|-------------|---------|
| Excellent | Specific claims about 2 CLOs; points to appropriate evidence (including Decision Logs, artifacts, Git history, Goal Document, and/or session review transcripts); honest evaluation of evidence strength ("this is strong because..." / "this is weaker because..."); genuine identification of areas for growth; can answer follow-up questions | 85--100 |
| Proficient | Claims present for 2 CLOs; evidence identified; some evaluation of evidence quality; follow-up answers adequate | 70--84 |
| Developing | General claims; vague evidence links ("I did stuff with Git"); no evaluation of evidence quality; struggles with follow-up questions | 50--69 |
| Insufficient | Cannot articulate claims; no evidence identified; cannot engage with follow-up | 0--49 |

## Connection to Session Review Speaking Practice

Students have been practising explaining their work to AI every week since Week 1. The session review is low-stakes rehearsal for this culminating conversation. By Week 13, explaining work orally is a well-practised skill, not a novel demand.

Week 12's session review scenario directly rehearses this: "AI plays an instructor asking about one of your Decision Log entries. Defend your self-assessment rating." AI role: Sceptical instructor.

## Logistics

At 5 min per student (4 min conversation + 1 min transition):

| Class size | Reflection time | Remaining for structured work |
|-----------|----------------|-------------------------------|
| 15 | 75 min | 70 min |
| 20 | 100 min | 45 min |
| 24 | 120 min | 25 min |

For classes above 20, the instructor may begin reflections during late Segment B (before break) and continue through Segment C. For classes of 24, the reflections consume most of the session; the structured work task must be self-directing.

### Structured Work During Reflections

Students not currently in a reflection work on a finalization checklist:

1. Run Accessibility Checker on your final artifact. Fix any issues.
2. Complete your process documentation (what AI did, what you changed, what you checked).
3. Review your peer response notes -- have you incorporated the feedback?
4. Prepare your sharing/presentation for Week 14 (1 min explanation of one design decision).

This checklist is a deliverable: students submit it (checked off, with notes) by end of session. It is ungraded but provides structure and accountability during the 100--120 minutes when the instructor is conducting reflections.
