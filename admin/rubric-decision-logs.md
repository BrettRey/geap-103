# GEAP 103 -- AI Decision Logs Rubric (30%)

**Course:** GEAP 103 Basic Computer Skills
**Assessment:** AI Decision Logs
**Weight:** 30% of final grade
**Primary CLOs:** 3 (Request formulation, prompt revision), 4 (Evaluation of AI outputs, decision documentation)

---

## Overview

Students submit 8 curated log entries selected from Weeks 2--11 as their best evidence of AI-assisted decision-making. Each entry includes a self-assessment. The entry window closes at Week 11 so the instructor can read and score the logs before conducting oral reflections in Week 13.

With 10 weeks of potential entries and a requirement of 8 curated ones, there is ample selection.

---

## Entry Template

Each entry follows this template. Student-authored text per entry (excluding pasted AI excerpt and self-assessment sentence): ~150--200 words.

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| Task goal + constraints | 1--2 sentences |
| AI used | Which AI tool and context (e.g., Copilot in Word, Claude, a coding agent, etc.) |
| Prompt snippet(s) | Max 3 turns (copy/paste or screenshot) |
| Output summary | What the AI proposed (1--2 sentences) |
| Decision | Accept / Modify / Reject |
| Verification | How they checked (ran it, used Accessibility Checker, tested on sample, etc.) |
| What changed | 1--2 bullets |
| **Self-assessment** | **Student rates own entry against rubric (Excellent / Proficient / Developing / Insufficient) + 1 sentence justifying the rating** |

---

## Grading Criteria

| Level | Description | % Range |
|-------|-------------|---------|
| Excellent | Clear and progressively ambitious goals across entries; systematic evaluation; substantive decisions with rationale; evidence of both exploration (trying new approaches) and exploitation (going deeper on what works); failure/rejection episodes present; self-assessments are well calibrated | 85--100 |
| Proficient | Goals stated with some ambition; evaluation present; decisions documented; entries show range of tasks attempted (not just 8 entries about the same trivial task); at least one failure/rejection episode; self-assessments present | 70--84 |
| Developing | Goals vague or consistently trivial; evaluation superficial; minimal decision rationale; little evidence of exploration or growth in ambition; self-assessments missing or poorly calibrated | 50--69 |
| Insufficient | Missing elements, no critical evaluation visible, no self-assessment | 0--49 |

*Note on ambition: "Ambitious" is calibrated to B1 -- it means the student attempted something that genuinely challenged their ability to articulate what they wanted, not something technically sophisticated. A student who struggles to describe a complex goal and documents that struggle is demonstrating more than a student who describes a trivial goal perfectly. The language is B1; the ambition can be high.*

---

## Scoring Heuristic

**Scoring heuristic:** (1) Read all 8 entries once for overall impression. (2) Check ambition trajectory: do goals get more challenging over time? (3) Check failure episode quality: is there at least one entry documenting AI output that was rejected or substantially revised? (4) Check self-assessment calibration: do the student's ratings roughly match what you'd give? (5) Assign a band.

## Annotated Exemplars

**Excellent exemplar (abridged):**

> Task goal: Create a budget spreadsheet that flags unusual spending. AI used: Copilot in Excel. Prompt: "Create a formula that highlights cells over $200 in red." Output: Copilot generated a conditional formatting rule but applied it to the wrong column. Decision: Modify. Verification: Checked by entering test data -- $250 in the food column was highlighted in the rent column instead. What changed: Revised prompt to specify the exact column range. Self-assessment: Proficient -- I caught the error and fixed it, but I should have specified the column in my first prompt.

*Why Excellent: Ambitious goal, specific verification, honest self-assessment that identifies a real improvement area.*

**Developing exemplar (abridged):**

> Task goal: Make a document. AI used: Copilot. Prompt: "Write a summary." Output: It wrote a summary. Decision: Accept. Verification: Looked at it. What changed: Nothing. Self-assessment: Excellent -- it was good.

*Why Developing: Vague goal, no specific verification, no evidence of evaluation, self-assessment is poorly calibrated.*

## Feedback Cycle

### Round 1: Entries 1--2

- **Submitted by:** ~Week 4
- **Feedback returned:** Week 6
- **Focus:** One strength, one specific improvement target
- Students are expected to identify in a subsequent entry what they changed based on feedback

### Round 2: Entries 3--5

- **Submitted by:** ~Week 8
- **Feedback returned:** Week 10--11
- **Focus:** Whether Round 1 feedback was incorporated
- Returned with confirmation signal ("target addressed" / "target still developing")

### Summative: Entries 6--8

- **Submitted:** Week 11 (8 curated entries + self-assessment, deadline 11:59 PM)
- **Scored:** Summatively using the rubric above
- No further feedback cycle; these entries are assessed as final evidence

---

## Self-Assessment Calibration

- Each entry includes a student self-rating (Excellent / Proficient / Developing / Insufficient) with a 1-sentence justification
- The instructor notes where student self-ratings diverge significantly from the instructor's assessment
- Patterns of over- or under-rating are discussed during the micro-defence circulation (brief one-on-one moment during triads)
- Self-assessment calibration patterns also inform the oral reflection conversations in Week 13

---

## Failure/Rejection Episode Expectations

Entries documenting cases where AI output was wrong or misleading are encouraged throughout. At least one such entry is expected across the 8 submissions, but all entries that document critical evaluation (including rejection or substantial revision of AI output) are valued. Quality of the evaluation matters more than the number of failure episodes.

Failure/rejection entries should show:

1. What the AI got wrong
2. How the student noticed
3. What they did instead

Generic or vague entries ("the AI made a mistake so I fixed it") should be flagged for revision at the next feedback round. The goal is authentic critical evaluation, not a compliance checkbox.

**Justified acceptance is also valued.** If a student develops genuinely good prompting skills and routinely gets acceptable output, entries documenting well-reasoned acceptance (with specific verification) are valid evidence. The rubric values the quality of the evaluation, not the decision outcome.

---

## Sample Feedback Comments

**Round 1 sample feedback (entries 1--2):**

- Strength: "You clearly described what you wanted AI to do and explained why the first attempt didn't work."
- Improvement target: "Your verification section says 'I checked it.' Be specific: what exactly did you check, and how? Did you compare numbers, read the text aloud, test it with sample data?"

**Round 2 sample feedback (entries 3--5), with confirmation signal:**

- "Target addressed: Your verification is now specific -- you described comparing formula results with manual calculation. Keep this level of detail."
- "Target still developing: You're still writing 'I checked it' without saying how. Try: I verified by _____."

---

## Authenticity Check

Compare the language in Decision Log entries against the student's demonstrated oral level at micro-defences. A marked mismatch (e.g., fluent academic prose from a student who struggles to sustain oral turns) warrants a brief oral follow-up: "Can you tell me in your own words what this Decision Log entry says?"

**Authenticity check note:** The written-vs-oral comparison is the primary authenticity mechanism for process documentation, but it has a limited shelf life as AI becomes capable of matching specific proficiency levels. The oral micro-defences and oral reflection are the most robust authenticity evidence. The written comparison catches obvious cases but should not be treated as foolproof.
