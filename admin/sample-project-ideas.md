# GEAP 103: Sample Project Ideas

**Purpose:** Curated project ideas for instructors and students. These are presented informally in Week 4 (project-type menu) and serve as inspiration throughout the course. Students are not limited to this list.

**Design principles** (from Mollick, Cummins, and Willison):
- **Personally meaningful:** Every project requires knowledge only the student has (their family, culture, neighbourhood, daily life). AI can't generate this without being told.
- **Identity-connected:** The student is positioned as expert, author, and cultural authority. Multilingualism is a resource, not a deficit. (Cummins)
- **Buildable with AI:** AI handles technical complexity. The student's job is articulation, evaluation, and revision. (Mollick)
- **Surprising:** Something the student didn't know was possible before the course. "I can build THAT?" (Willison)
- **Shareable:** Every project produces something with a URL or a file that the student would want to show family, friends, or an employer.
- **Language-intensive:** The articulation challenge scales with ambition. Vague descriptions produce generic results. Precise descriptions produce useful ones.

**Sources:** Inspired by the work of Ethan Mollick (AI as creative partner, "Co-Intelligence"), Jim Cummins (identity texts, linguistic interdependence), and Simon Willison (practical AI-built tools, tools.simonwillison.net).

## Tier 1: High Identity, High Surprise

These combine personal meaning with unexpected technical ambition. Best for students who invest in the articulation challenge.

### 1. Bilingual Children's Storybook
Write and illustrate (AI image generation) a short children's book in English and your home language. Retell a family story or traditional tale. Printable PDF with facing-page translations. **Language challenge:** Simple prose for children is deceptively hard at B1; AI image prompts must be precise enough for consistent characters across 8-12 pages; translation choices require metalinguistic explanation.

### 2. Family Recipe Archive
A small website presenting 5-8 family recipes with photos, cultural context, and personal stories. Search function, ingredient glossary, bilingual content. **Language challenge:** Describing cooking techniques, textures, and flavours precisely; translating culturally specific food concepts; writing narrative headnotes in the student's own voice.

### 3. "Before and After" Migration Story (Scrollytelling)
A scrolling narrative webpage telling the story of your (or your family's) move to Canada. Two-column before/after structure with images, text, and embedded media. **Language challenge:** Sustaining coherent narrative across 800-1,200 words; past tenses for extended storytelling; articulating emotional and cultural contrasts.

### 4. Neighbourhood Walking Tour
A guided audio tour of your neighbourhood with a map interface. 8-10 stops that matter personally: the first place you ate in Canada, the library where you studied, the shop where someone first spoke your language. **Language challenge:** Writing a tour script for a listener who has never visited; describing spatial relationships and visual landmarks; speaking for a recorded audience.

### 5. "What My Name Means" Multimedia Presentation
Explore the meaning, origin, and story of your name(s). Etymology, cultural naming practices, personal narrative about your name in Canadian contexts. **Language challenge:** Cultural explanation for an unfamiliar audience; metalinguistic skill of explaining linguistic features of your home language in English.

### 6. Elder Interview: Oral History Recording
Record a 10-15 min interview with an elder family or community member. Provide an edited transcript, biographical introduction, and reflective essay. Interview can be in the home language with English subtitles/translation. **Language challenge:** Preparing interview questions, summarizing spoken narrative, translation/paraphrase, reflective writing about what the interview revealed.

## Tier 2: Practical + Personal

These solve real problems in the student's daily life. Best for students motivated by usefulness.

### 7. Personal Budget Dashboard
Interactive budget tracker tailored to your actual financial situation as a student in Toronto. AI generates formulas and charts; you define categories, priorities, and conditional logic ("If my phone bill is over $50, highlight it red"). **Language challenge:** Specifying financial categories and conditional logic in plain English; explaining budgeting priorities clearly enough for AI to implement.

### 8. Community Resource Finder
A clickable map or app prototype that helps newcomers find specific resources: halal groceries, free English classes, worship spaces, transit tips. Each pin includes a written review and practical tip. **Language challenge:** Writing concise, informative descriptions; categorizing resources; specifying UI requirements to AI.

### 9. Timezone Meeting Planner
Enter cities where your family lives. See everyone's local time. Drag a slider to find a time that works for all. **Language challenge:** Describing constraints precisely ("My mother works until 6 PM in Manila, my sister has class until 3 PM in Dubai, and I am free after 5 PM in Toronto").

### 10. "What I Wish I'd Known" Newcomer Guide
A practical, opinionated digital guide for newcomers from your specific country arriving in your specific Canadian city. Not generic; based on real experience. **Language challenge:** Writing practical advice clearly; choosing the right level of directness; differentiating facts from recommendations.

### 11. Apartment Comparison Tool
A decision matrix for rental listings against your personal criteria: transit time, cost, neighbourhood, proximity to amenities. AI builds the scoring system; you define and weight the criteria. **Language challenge:** Defining abstract criteria precisely; explaining trade-offs; iterating when the scoring produces unexpected results.

### 12. Transit Schedule Visualizer
A personal commute plan showing when to leave, which bus to catch, buffer time, and the "last safe bus" for each class. Built around your specific schedule. **Language challenge:** Describing temporal sequences and spatial relationships; specifying constraints and fallback options.

## Tier 3: Creative + Ambitious

These push toward genuinely surprising artifacts. Best for students who want to explore what AI makes possible.

### 13. Playable Text Adventure Game
A browser-based text game set in a world drawn from your experience or culture. AI generates code; you write all narrative, dialogue, and puzzle logic. **Language challenge:** Writing branching dialogue; giving unambiguous instructions to the player; describing settings vividly in text only; specifying game logic to AI.

### 14. Interactive Quiz Game
A browser-based quiz on a topic you know well (your country's geography, a sport, cooking). Multiple choice, scoring, timer, feedback. Shareable link. **Language challenge:** Writing clear, unambiguous questions with one correct answer; describing game flow and scoring rules; specifying visual style.

### 15. Data Story: Visualizing Something You Care About
Find a public dataset (air quality, immigration stats, tuition trends) and build a 3-5 chart data story with narrative annotations. **Language challenge:** Describing patterns accurately; writing hedged causal claims; giving AI precise instructions about what to plot and label.

### 16. Podcast Pilot (3 Episodes)
Plan, script, and record a 3-episode podcast on a topic you know well. AI helps with music, editing, cover art, and show notes. You write all scripts and do all speaking. **Language challenge:** Writing conversational but structured scripts; speaking fluently for a real audience; describing the desired "feel" to AI.

### 17. Custom Language-Learning Tool
A web app that helps someone learn basic phrases in your first language, with pronunciation guides, cultural usage notes, and a quiz. You are the language expert; AI builds the tool. **Language challenge:** Explaining pronunciation and grammar of your L1 in English; writing usage notes; specifying interactive behaviour.

### 18. "Explain My Field" Video Series
Scripts and AI-generated slide decks for 3 short explainer videos about key concepts in your field of study. You record the voiceover. **Language challenge:** Explaining technical concepts accessibly; structuring explanations logically; controlling pace and pronunciation.

## Tier 4: Professional + Portfolio-Building

These produce artifacts with direct professional value.

### 19. Career Exploration Portfolio Website
Research 3-4 career paths, build a portfolio site with personal statement, skill inventory, and annotated links to Canadian job postings. **Language challenge:** Writing professional self-description; articulating skills using Canadian workplace vocabulary; evaluating and revising AI-drafted professional language.

### 20. Family Business Website
A functional website for a real or planned family business or side hustle. About page, services, pricing, contact, testimonials. **Language challenge:** Professional and promotional writing; describing services clearly; writing in a business register.

### 21. Community Organization Profile
Professional promotional materials (flyer, social media posts, web page) for a real community organization the student is connected to. Includes an interview with someone from the organization. **Language challenge:** Professional/promotional register; persuasive writing; summarizing interview content; writing for different formats.

## Combination Projects

Students can combine types for higher ambition:
- Budget dashboard (#7) + OCR receipt scanner = automatic expense tracking
- Neighbourhood guide (#8) + walking tour (#4) = comprehensive newcomer resource
- Recipe archive (#2) + children's storybook (#1) = bilingual family heritage project
- Data story (#15) + podcast (#16) = multimedia investigative report

## Assessment Note

These ideas are presented as inspiration, not requirements. Assessment is on the communicative package (articulation + evaluation + documentation + sharing), not on the technical sophistication of the artifact. A student who builds a simple budget spreadsheet with excellent process documentation and honest gap-naming scores higher than a student who builds an elaborate game with no documentation.

The language level is B1; the project ambition can be high. Assessment rewards students who stretch their ability to articulate what they want, not students who execute trivial tasks perfectly.
