# GEAP 103: What This Course Is

**For:** Instructors preparing to teach GEAP 103 Basic Computer Skills
**Date:** 2026-02-12
**Status:** Working document

---

## The one-sentence version

GEAP 103 teaches students to say what they mean clearly enough to get things done with a computer. The computer skills are real, but English is the control layer.

---

## What changed

Until recently, "basic computer skills" meant learning applications. Open Excel. Click cell B2. Type `=SUM(A1:A10)`. Students who could follow those steps had "computer skills."

That world is gone. AI systems can now take a plain-English description of what you want and build it. Not toy versions -- working applications, data tools, documents, visualizations. The bottleneck is no longer "do you know which menu to click?" The bottleneck is "can you describe what you want precisely enough for AI to build it?"

This changes what "basic computer skills" means. The basic skill is now articulation: saying what you want, evaluating whether you got it, and revising when you didn't. That's an English skill.

---

## What this means for September

This course launches in September 2026. AI capabilities are advancing faster than curriculum cycles. By September, students may be able to:

- Say "build me a budget tracker that pulls data from my bank" and get a working tool
- Say "create a presentation about my program for my family" and get a slide deck that's better than what most professionals produce
- Say "I have this data about Ontario college enrollment -- show me the patterns" and get interactive visualizations

These aren't science fiction. Some of them are possible today. By September, more will be. A course that teaches students to format cells in Excel is teaching them to sharpen a pencil in the age of voice dictation.

But here's what WON'T change by September: the need to say what you mean. "Make me something good" will still produce garbage. "I need a one-page summary of this article for B1 English learners, with simple vocabulary, organized by the three main arguments, with key terms bolded" will still produce something useful. The difference is English proficiency, not computer proficiency.

## The traps

If you've taught computer skills before (or taken a computer skills course, or imagined what one looks like), you will fall into these traps. They're natural. Recognizing them is the first step.

### Trap 1: Organizing around tools

**What it looks like:** "This week we learn Excel. Next week we learn PowerPoint."

**Why it's wrong:** The tools change. Excel might be irrelevant by the time you're teaching. What doesn't change: the challenge of making sense of data, or communicating information to an audience. Organize around challenges, not tools. "This week: can you make sense of numbers?" The tool is whatever gets them there.

### Trap 2: Organizing around artifacts

**What it looks like:** "What are you going to build?" as the central question. Evaluating students by what they produced. Treating the project as the destination.

**Why it's wrong:** The course teaches English and tool discovery. The artifact is a vehicle for practising articulation, evaluation, and reflection. A student who produced a mediocre spreadsheet but can explain clearly what they wanted, what went wrong, and what they'd do differently has learned more than a student who produced a beautiful dashboard but can't explain it.

The question isn't "What did you build?" The question is "Can you say what you mean?"

### Trap 3: Setting the ceiling too low

**What it looks like:** "Make a simple budget spreadsheet." "Create a 5-slide presentation." "Write a one-page document with headings."

**Why it's wrong:** These are trivial tasks for AI. A student can accomplish any of them in their first week with no instruction. If these are the challenges, students have nothing to struggle with, and struggle is where language development happens.

Think bigger. "Build me a spreadsheet with built-in API support." "Create an app for my phone that identifies location by sound." "Figure out why Ontario colleges can't publish their data efficiently and propose a solution." Students at B1 may not be able to articulate goals this ambitious yet -- but that's the point. The course develops their ability to articulate increasingly complex things. If the target is "rename a file," there's nothing to develop.

### Trap 4: Teaching steps instead of judgment

**What it looks like:** "Step 1: Click File. Step 2: Click New. Step 3: Select Template."

**Why it's wrong:** AI handles steps. Students can say "create a new document from the report template" and it happens. What AI can't do is decide WHETHER to use a template, WHICH template fits the purpose, and WHETHER the result is any good. Those are judgment calls. Judgment requires understanding the problem, which requires being able to articulate it, which is English.

Your job as an instructor is to develop judgment, not teach procedures.

### Trap 5: Treating the Goal Document as a project plan

**What it looks like:** "By Week 4, you should have your project idea." "What's your plan?" "Is this feasible?"

**Why it's wrong:** The Goal Document is a language exercise. Students practise expressing desires (Week 1), evaluating results (Week 2), describing imagined possibilities (Week 3), and reflecting on how their thinking has changed (Week 4). The arc is richer articulation, not project convergence.

Some students will have consistent interests across four weeks. Some will change direction completely. Both are good. Changing your mind after trying things is what learning looks like. If you pressure students toward commitment, you kill the exploration that makes the language practice meaningful.

## What "good" looks like

### Good instructor moves

- "What did you want? What did you get? Where's the gap?" (focuses on articulation)
- "Can you explain that to your partner? Did they understand?" (tests whether language works)
- "What would you ask for differently next time?" (develops revision skills)
- "That's an interesting idea. Can you describe it more specifically? What would someone see if they looked at it?" (pushes articulation toward precision)
- "You changed your mind since last week. What happened?" (values reflection, not consistency)

### Bad instructor moves

- "Let me show you how to do that in Excel." (teaching steps)
- "That project idea is too ambitious." (setting a ceiling)
- "You need to pick a project type." (forcing premature commitment)
- "Your spreadsheet should have three columns." (specifying the artifact)
- "That's not what I asked for." (when the student's interpretation reveals something interesting about how they understood the problem)

### What to watch for in students

**A student who is learning:** Can describe what they wanted in progressively more precise language across the semester. Can explain the gap between what they asked for and what they got. Can articulate why they changed their approach. Uses technical vocabulary accurately when explaining their work orally.

**A student who is stuck:** Keeps saying "it didn't work" without being able to say what they expected or what went wrong. Can't explain their work to a partner. Copies AI output without evaluating it. Says "I don't know what I want" in Week 8 (in Week 2, this is fine and expected).

## The two course goals

Everything in this course serves exactly two goals:

1. **English language development.** Students practise expressing desires, evaluating outcomes, describing possibilities, reflecting on change, interpreting technical text, explaining technical work orally, giving and receiving feedback, and writing structured documentation. All of this is English at B1.

2. **Tool discovery.** Students discover what they can do with a computer -- not by being told, but by trying things, failing, revising, and trying again. The specific tools matter less than the discovery process. By the end of the semester, students should have a realistic sense of what's possible and the language skills to pursue it.

These goals reinforce each other. You can't discover tools without being able to articulate what you want. You can't develop articulation without having something real to talk about. The course is the intersection.

---

## What you don't need to be

You don't need to be a technology expert. You don't need to know Excel formulas or Git commands or how to write code. AI handles all of that. What you need to be is someone who can:

- Hear what a student is trying to say and help them say it more clearly
- Ask "What did you want? What did you get?" and listen to the answer
- Recognize when a student's language is developing (from "I want something good" to "I need a document that explains X to Y audience with Z constraints")
- Push students toward ambition ("What if you tried something harder?")
- Celebrate when students change their minds ("You discovered your first idea wasn't interesting. That's learning.")

You are a language teacher. The computer is the context.

---

## A note on ambition

The course is called "Basic Computer Skills." Don't let the title fool you. By September, there may be nothing basic about what students can accomplish with AI. The constraint is not technical capability. The constraint is whether a B1 English learner can describe what they want clearly enough.

Your job is to expand what they can describe. The computer will handle the rest.

No special software needed. No Copilot licence needed.

You'll know what the error messages look like, what students will struggle with, and what to say when they get stuck — because you got stuck first.
