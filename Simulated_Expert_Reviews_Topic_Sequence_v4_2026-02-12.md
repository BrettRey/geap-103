## Review 1: Rod Ellis (Curtin University) -- TBLT

### What is strong

The shift from tool-focused to challenge-focused organization addresses a structural problem I would have continued to press on. When the weekly question is "What can a computer do for me?" rather than "Learn OneDrive," the learner has a reason to use language to solve a problem. That is closer to the conditions under which acquisition occurs.

The Goal Document thread is, in my judgement, the single best design decision in this version. It creates a longitudinal task chain with genuine communicative need. The genre remains constant while cognitive and linguistic demands increase: from a wish list (Week 1) to risk assessment (Week 8) to evaluative reflection (Week 14). This is principled complexity grading within a single task type. Because the content is personal, learners cannot copy each other's output. By mid-semester, students are comparing what they planned with what they built -- a comparison demanding subordination, hedging, and evaluative language that sits squarely within B1.

The fluency strategy (Decision Logs 8x, micro-defences 3x, Goal Document 12x) operationalizes the principle that procedural knowledge develops through repeated performance of the same task type with varying content. Attentional resources shift from task management to language production as the structure becomes familiar.

My previous concerns about CLO 5/6 timing, deliberate mismatch, and pair work distribution have been adequately addressed. The Git concern has been overtaken by the redesign.

### What concerns me

**Planning time remains unspecified.** Pre-task planning (even 5-10 minutes) increases complexity and fluency of output without reducing accuracy, particularly at intermediate levels. For micro-defences especially, the absence of a planning phase is a missed opportunity.

**Weeks 11-14 are underspecified.** This is the phase where task complexity should be highest and collaborative tasks introduce the interactional demands (negotiation of meaning, clarification requests, confirmation checks) most productive for acquisition. Without specified collaborative roles, stronger students may dominate production while weaker students contribute minimally.

**The Goal Document needs a feedback loop.** Ungraded is philosophically sound but pedagogically risky. At B1, learners are typically assessment-oriented. Without instructor comments or peer response, some students will treat it as optional. Its fluency-building potential depends on the learner perceiving it as communicative.

**Complexity grading across Goal Document entries should be made explicit.** The progression from wish list to reflection is implicit in the prompts. Making it explicit would strengthen the design and help other instructors implement consistently.

### Recommendations

1. Add 5-10 minute pre-task planning phase to Segment B, particularly for micro-defence weeks and the mid-semester artefact.
2. Elaborate Weeks 11-14 with collaborative roles, information-gap structures, and mechanisms ensuring all learners engage in meaning negotiation.
3. Build a minimal feedback loop into the Goal Document: instructor comments at the three reading points and/or structured peer response.
4. Specify the complexity variables that increase across Goal Document entries in terms of reasoning demands, number of elements, and degree of justification required.

## Review 2: Greg Wilson (Teaching Tech Together) -- Computing for non-programmers

### What is strong

The challenge-focused restructuring is genuine, not cosmetic. "Today we make something someone else can actually use" produces a different class than "Today we learn Word." The tool schedule confirms this: tools appear when challenges demand them, and six of fourteen weeks introduce no new tool.

The vocabulary pre-training is the single most important change from v1. Five terms per session, pre-trained, across nine weeks gives ~40 terms with spaced repetition built in by recurring task types. Concrete and auditable.

Pair work every week from Week 1, with collaboration challenge in Week 7 and project in Week 11, is an honest distribution. The three-segment session structure is realistic for three hours with B1 learners.

### What concerns me

**Goal Document prompts need sentence frames.** A2+ students have ideas but may lack the English. Week 7 asks students to write a GitHub Issue. That's a genre-specific task. Provide templates: "I want to build _____ because _____. To do this I need to learn _____."

**Weeks 9-10 need bounded project types.** "Mini worked examples by project type" is a promise, not a plan. Without a menu of 3-4 types with worked examples, the instructor must improvise differentiated instruction for 15-24 students. That's not a teaching plan; it's a hope.

**Week 8 troubleshooting should include a Git failure case.** PRs are introduced in Week 7. The troubleshooting worked examples should include at least one Git-specific case (e.g., AI committed the wrong file, a merge went sideways).

**Excel may benefit from a reading-first approach.** Reading and understanding an existing spreadsheet is a different skill from building one. Consider having the worked example start with interpreting a spreadsheet someone else made before building one.

**Write the instructor guide before the student materials.** The design works only if the person teaching it gets detailed session plans, not just a topic sequence.

### Recommendations

1. Provide sentence frames for every Goal Document prompt.
2. Constrain project types to 3-4. Publish the menu in Week 4. Write one worked example per type.
3. Include a Git failure case in Week 8 troubleshooting.
4. In Week 6, start with reading/interpreting an existing spreadsheet before building one.
5. Prioritize the instructor guide in the development plan.

## Review 3: Richard Mayer (UC Santa Barbara) -- Cognitive load

### What is strong

All five original concerns have been addressed with solutions well grounded in learning science. Worked examples are specified for every new-skill week. Vocabulary pre-training moves terms to the opening segment. The three-segment structure with a break addresses the monolithic-block problem. Git pre-training opens with a visual timeline. The challenge-focused framing is a direct application of the coherence principle: tools appear because problems demand them, reducing "why are we learning this?" load.

The deliberate-mismatch design in Week 2 turns what could be passive demonstration into active comparison, which the generation-effect literature suggests should strengthen learning.

### What concerns me

**Goal Document prompt alignment.** In weeks where the prompt extends the day's challenge (Weeks 5-7), the Segment C pivot from application to Goal Document is connective. In weeks where the prompt is more generic (Weeks 1, 8), it may compete with consolidation of the day's learning. The risk is modest at 10-15 minutes but present.

**Week 9 mini worked examples.** Multiple brief demonstrations sorted by project type create a split-attention risk: students watching irrelevant demonstrations process material that doesn't advance their goal. The coherence principle suggests learners should see only the example matching their project type.

**Decision Log and Goal Document overlap.** Both ask students to write reflectively about their work. In weeks where both are assigned, verify they prompt for distinct cognitive operations. Where they overlap, merge or alternate.

### Recommendations

1. Align every Goal Document prompt with the day's challenge. Revise generic prompts to function as transfer tasks.
2. In Week 9, have students self-select into project-type groups and view only the relevant example. A gallery walk afterward can expose them to other approaches without sustained attention.
3. Audit DL/GD overlap week by week. Note that students choose which weeks to write DL entries (8 of 12 weeks), so overlap is intermittent, but differentiate the prompts clearly where both occur.
4. In early semesters, monitor whether the Segment C pivot feels disruptive or connective. If disruptive, consider moving Goal Document to the following week's Segment A as a retrieval-practice warm-up.
