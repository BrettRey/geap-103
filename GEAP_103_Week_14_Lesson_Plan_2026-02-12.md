# GEAP 103 Week 14 Lesson Plan: Did I Build What I Wanted?

**Context:** 3 x 55 min (150 min instruction + 15-min break), B1 learners, BYOD, Office 365 + Copilot
**Week 14 of 14** | Phase 3: Creation (final week)

**Learning Objectives (Week 14):**
1. Finalise and submit a final Artifact Package (product + process documentation + verification note + sharing evidence) (CLO 6)
2. Finalise and submit Git Episodes (3 required episodes with evidence) (CLOs 1, 7)
3. Finalise and submit an Evidence-Indexed Reflection with claims, evidence, and honest evaluation for all 7 CLOs (all CLOs)
4. Explain what you built, what you learned, and how your goals changed, in an oral presentation to the class (CLOs 2, 5)

**No new vocabulary.** The 40 terms introduced in Weeks 1-9 should be part of active vocabulary by now. Students will use them naturally in presentations and sharing.

**Goal Thread -- Final Reflection (Oral):**
There is no written Goal Document entry this week. The Goal Document prompt ("Is this what I set out to make? What changed?") is answered orally during the informal sharing in Segment B. This is the resolution of the Goal Document arc: 12 written entries developing from "I wish my computer could..." to "When I started, I thought... Now I think..." to this final oral moment where students say, out loud, whether they built what they wanted and what changed along the way. The shift from written to oral is deliberate. The course ends with the student's voice, not their writing.

## Segment A: Final Submissions (0:00--0:40)

### 0:00--0:05 | Framing
**Format:** Whole class
**Language skills:** Listening

"This is the last class. Three things are due today: your final Artifact Package, your Git Episodes, and your Evidence-Indexed Reflection. You'll have the first 35 minutes to finish and submit everything. After that, we share."

"A reminder about what each submission includes."

Display submission checklist:

| Submission | What's included | Where to submit |
|-----------|----------------|----------------|
| **Final Artifact Package** | The artifact itself + process documentation (own words) + verification note + evidence of sharing | [Instructor's established process] |
| **Git Episodes** | Evidence of 3 episodes: Restore, Error Recovery, Collaboration (screenshots, logs, links) | [Instructor's established process] |
| **Evidence-Indexed Reflection** | For each of 7 CLOs: 1 claim + evidence links + evidence evaluation + growth area (~600-800 words total) | [Instructor's established process] |

"If your Artifact Package isn't perfect, that's fine. The documentation matters as much as the product. If your reflection is honest about where your evidence is weak, that's better than a reflection that claims everything is excellent."

### 0:05--0:10 | Pre-Task Planning
**Format:** Individual
**Language skills:** Reading (own checklist, own drafts)

"Take 5 minutes. Look at your three submissions. What's done? What still needs work? Write a quick priority list: what you'll finish first, second, third. If something is already done, submit it now and move on."

Students assess their own readiness and prioritise. Some students will have completed most of their work during Week 13. Others will need most of this time.

### 0:10--0:40 | Finalisation and Submission
**Format:** Individual work with instructor circulation
**Language skills:** Writing (process documentation, verification notes, reflection), reading (own portfolio, AI output, system feedback)

Students finalise and submit. Three parallel tasks, in whatever order the student needs:

**1. Final Artifact Package.** Complete any remaining work on the artifact. Write or revise the verification note: what was checked, what was found, what was not completed. Ensure process documentation is in the student's own words. Compile evidence of sharing (Week 12 peer feedback, collaborative work, any other sharing).

**2. Git Episodes.** Compile evidence for the three required episodes (Restore, Error Recovery, Collaboration). If a student is missing an episode, this is the last chance to generate evidence -- but fabricating evidence is worse than submitting an incomplete set. A student who honestly documents two clear episodes and admits the third is missing will score higher than a student who submits three vague screenshots.

**3. Evidence-Indexed Reflection.** Complete the remaining CLO entries (students should have 3-4 drafted from Week 13). Revise evidence evaluations based on Week 13 peer feedback. Ensure growth areas are specific, not generic.

Instructor circulates with three purposes:
1. **Submission support:** Help students compile and submit. Solve access or format issues.
2. **Reflection quality check:** "Can you read me your evidence evaluation for CLO ___? Does it actually say how strong the evidence is, or does it just describe the evidence?"
3. **Reassurance:** Some students will be anxious. The message is: submit what you have. Honest documentation of what you did and didn't achieve is the skill.

Students who finish early: practise their informal presentation (Segment B). Think about what they want to show and say.

## Segment B: Sharing (0:40--1:35)

### 0:40--0:45 | Pre-Task Planning for Presentations
**Format:** Individual reflection
**Language skills:** Reading (own artifact, own Goal Document), writing (brief notes)

"In a few minutes, you'll share what you built with the class. This isn't graded. It's a celebration. But even celebrations benefit from a moment of planning."

"Think about three things:
1. What will you show? (Your artifact, your portfolio, your favourite Decision Log entry -- whatever you're most proud of.)
2. What will you say about what you learned?
3. Is this what you set out to make? What changed?"

Students take brief notes. This is not a script. It's organising thoughts before speaking.

### 0:45--1:10 | Individual Sharing
**Format:** Whole class, informal presentations (2-3 min each)
**Language skills:** Speaking (sustained, personal, evaluative), listening (peers' presentations)

Students take turns sharing informally with the class. This is not a formal presentation. No slides required (though students can show their artifact on screen). The format is conversational: show something, say something, answer a question.

**Structure per student (~2-3 min):**
1. Show what you built (30-60 sec)
2. Say what you learned (30-60 sec)
3. Answer: "Is this what you set out to make? What changed along the way?" (30-60 sec)

Instructor and classmates can ask one brief follow-up question.

**Pacing:** With 15-20 students at 2-3 min each, this takes 30-60 min. If the class is large, some students present in Segment B and others in Segment C. Alternatively, split the class into two groups presenting simultaneously in different parts of the room, with the instructor moving between them.

**Instructor note on tone:** This is celebratory. Do not grade these. Do not take notes on a rubric. Listen. React. Ask genuine questions. If a student says "I didn't finish what I planned, but I learned a lot," that's a perfect answer. The goal is for students to hear themselves articulate what they've accomplished. For many, this will be the first time they've explained a technology project to an audience in English. That's the achievement.

### 1:10--1:35 | Collaborative Group Presentations
**Format:** Group presentations (3-5 min per group)
**Language skills:** Speaking (role-based: introducing, demonstrating, evaluating), listening (other groups)

Each collaborative group presents their artifact. Roles are visible:

- **Coordinator** introduces the project: what they set out to build, how they divided the work, what the timeline looked like
- **Builder** demonstrates the artifact: shows it working, points out key features, describes what AI did and what the group did
- **Reviewer** evaluates what worked and what didn't: what they would change, what they're proud of, what the quality issues were

**Structure per group (~3-5 min):**
1. Coordinator introduction (1 min)
2. Builder demonstration (1-2 min)
3. Reviewer evaluation (1 min)
4. One question from the class (1 min)

"Every role involves different language. The Coordinator plans and summarises. The Builder describes and shows. The Reviewer evaluates and judges. All three are language skills you'll use in your degree programs."

**Pacing:** With 6-8 groups at 3-5 min each, this takes 20-40 min. Adjust based on class size. If time is tight, shorten to 3 min per group strictly.

## Segment C: Wrap-Up (1:50--2:45)

### 1:50--2:00 | Overflow Presentations (if needed)
**Format:** Same as Segment B
**Language skills:** Same as Segment B

If individual or group presentations didn't finish in Segment B, complete them here. If all presentations are done, skip to the next section.

### 2:00--2:20 | "What Can You Do Now That You Couldn't in Week 1?"
**Format:** Whole class discussion, then pair work
**Language skills:** Speaking (evaluative, comparative), listening (peers and instructor)

"Let me ask you the question the whole course has been building toward. Not about your artifact. Not about your grade. About you. What can you do now that you couldn't do in Week 1?"

Instructor starts with a concrete observation: "In Week 1, most of you had never heard the word 'repository.' Today you presented projects that you built collaboratively using shared repositories. That's real."

**Pair discussion (5 min):** Students pair up and tell each other one thing they can do now that they couldn't before. Not a big, abstract answer -- a specific thing. "I can write a help request that someone can actually use." "I can tell Copilot what I want and fix it when it's wrong." "I can work on a project with other people without losing each other's work."

**Whole-class sharing (5 min):** Volunteers share. Instructor writes responses on the board (or a shared document).

**Instructor note:** This is a language moment disguised as a wrap-up activity. Students are producing evaluative, comparative language ("I can now..., which I couldn't...") orally, without sentence frames, in a low-stakes setting. If the course worked, they can do this. If they struggle, that's useful information too.

### 2:20--2:30 | Transfer to Degree Programs
**Format:** Instructor-led, whole class
**Language skills:** Listening

"You're moving into your degree programs. The specific tools might change. The skills don't."

Briefly connect course skills to what students will encounter:

| What you learned here | Where it shows up next |
|----------------------|----------------------|
| Telling AI what you want (prompting, revising) | Every course that allows AI tools |
| Evaluating whether output is right (Decision Logs) | Research, assignments, professional work |
| Writing help requests and documentation | Group projects, IT support, workplace communication |
| Working on shared projects with version control | Any collaborative assignment, co-op placements |
| Explaining your work to others (micro-defences) | Presentations, job interviews, professional meetings |
| Organising files and versions | Every course, every job |

"The one thing I hope you take from this course is that English is the control layer. If you can say what you mean clearly, you can get a computer to help you do almost anything. The skill isn't the computer. The skill is the language."

### 2:30--2:40 | Course Evaluation
**Format:** Individual
**Language skills:** Writing (evaluative)

Students complete the institutional course evaluation. Standard process per Humber policy.

Instructor steps out or turns away while students complete evaluations (standard practice).

### 2:40--2:45 | Closing
**Format:** Whole class

"You started this course 14 weeks ago. Some of you didn't know what a file path was. Some of you had never used AI. All of you have now built something real, documented how you built it, shared it with others, and explained it in English. That's what this course was about."

"Your final submissions are in. Your Decision Logs are in. Your Goal Documents tell the story of how your thinking changed. I've enjoyed reading them."

"Good luck in your degree programs. You're ready."

## After Class

- **Grade final Artifact Packages (10%).** Use the Artifact Package rubric from Assessment Framework v4. Focus on: ambition relative to B1, quality of process documentation (in student's own words), verification note honesty, evidence of sharing. Compare against the mid-semester artifact (Week 10): is there visible growth in ambition or documentation quality?
- **Grade Git Episodes (10%).** Checklist-based: 3 required episodes (Restore, Error Recovery, Collaboration). Short oral confirmation for any suspiciously "perfect" evidence (can be done via email or brief meeting during exam period if needed).
- **Grade Evidence-Indexed Reflections (10%).** Look for: specific claims (not generic), appropriate evidence linked, honest evaluation of evidence strength, genuine growth areas. Flag reflections that evaluate all evidence as "strong" -- that's a calibration issue, not a quality one.
- **Return final Goal Document comments.** Brief comments on the full 13-entry arc. This is the third and final reading.
- **Calculate final grades.** Components: Decision Logs (30%), Oral Micro-Defences (25%), Artifact Packages (25%), Git Episodes (10%), Evidence-Indexed Reflection (10%).
- **Flag any students whose process documentation language is markedly different from their micro-defence oral level** for a brief oral follow-up (per Assessment Framework v4 guidance on authenticity).

## Language Development Summary

- **Vocabulary:** No new terms. All 40 terms from Weeks 1-9 deployed naturally in presentations, reflections, and discussions. This week is the best evidence of whether vocabulary has been acquired: students use terms without prompting, in oral and written contexts, across multiple genres.
- **Speaking:** Individual informal presentations (2-3 min sustained: showing, explaining, evaluating); collaborative group presentations with role-based speaking (Coordinator introduces, Builder demonstrates, Reviewer evaluates); pair discussion of growth ("What can you do now?"); whole-class sharing of achievements
- **Reading:** Own portfolio (final review during submission); submission checklist; transfer table; course evaluation
- **Writing:** Final Artifact Package components (process documentation, verification note); Evidence-Indexed Reflection (completing remaining CLO entries); Git Episode documentation; course evaluation
- **Listening:** Peers' individual and group presentations; instructor's transfer discussion; whole-class sharing of achievements; closing remarks

## Assessment Alignment

- **Final Artifact Package due (10% of final grade):** Product + process documentation in student's own words + verification note + evidence of sharing. This is the most complex and ambitious of the three artifact packages. Assessed on the communicative package, not just the product. (CLOs 1, 5, 6)
- **Git Episodes due (10% of final grade):** Evidence for 3 required episodes (Restore, Error Recovery, Collaboration). Checklist-based assessment. (CLOs 1, 7)
- **Evidence-Indexed Reflection due (10% of final grade):** For each of 7 CLOs: claim + evidence + evidence evaluation + growth area. ~600-800 words. Assessed on specificity of claims, appropriateness of evidence, honesty of evaluation, and genuine identification of growth areas. (All CLOs)
- **Goal Document:** No written entry this week. The Goal Document prompt ("Is this what I set out to make? What changed?") is answered orally during informal sharing. Instructor returns brief final comments on the full 13-entry arc.
- **Informal presentations:** Not graded. These serve as evidence of oral fluency development and as a celebratory culmination. Instructor may note observations for the B1.2 exit evidence portfolio but does not score these presentations.
- **Course complete.** Final grades calculated from: Decision Logs 30% + Oral Micro-Defences 25% + Artifact Packages 25% (7% + 8% + 10%) + Git Episodes 10% + Evidence-Indexed Reflection 10%.
