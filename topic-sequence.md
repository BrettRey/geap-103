# GEAP 103: 14-Week Topic Sequence (Draft v4, revised)

**Course:** GEAP 103 Basic Computer Skills (Foundation Semester, B1)
**Version:** Draft v4, 2026-02-12
**Arc:** Discovery (Weeks 1-4) → Building (Weeks 5-10) → Creation (Weeks 11-14)
**Contact time:** 3 hours/week (42 hours total)
**Environment:** Office 365 + Copilot, BYOD

---

## The Two Threads

### Challenge thread (weekly focus)

Each week poses a question or challenge that the class works on together. The challenge is what drives instruction, practice, and assessment.

### Goal thread (running throughout)

Students maintain a personal **Goal Document** from Week 1 onward: a weekly language exercise where they practise saying what they want, evaluating what happened when they tried, describing possibilities, and reflecting on how their thinking has changed. The Goal Document is not a project plan. It is where students develop the language skills of goal articulation, evaluation, speculation, and reflection. Artifacts eventually emerge from this thinking, but the thinking -- and the language that enables it -- is the point.

The instructor reads the Goal Document at **Weeks 4, 8, and 13** and provides brief written comments (1-3 sentences) on clarity of thinking and language development. Structured peer response occurs at **Weeks 4, 8, and 12**. This loop keeps the document communicative without grading it.

| Phase | Goal thread stage | Key question | Complexity level |
|-------|------------------|--------------|-----------------|
| Discovery (1-4) | Finding language for wanting, evaluating, imagining, reflecting | Can I say what I mean? What interests me? | **Listing and describing:** simple sentences, concrete vocabulary, personal preferences |
| Building (5-8) | Narrowing and scoping | What specifically will I build? What do I need to learn? | **Planning and justifying:** conditional language, technical vocabulary, reasoning about feasibility |
| Creation (9-14) | Executing and refining | Am I building what I intended? Does it do what I wanted? | **Evaluating and comparing:** subordination, hedging, evaluative language, gap analysis between intention and result |

## Design Principles

1. **Challenge-focused, not tool-focused.** Each week is organized around a problem or question. Tools are introduced because they help solve the problem.
2. **Recurring task types build fluency.** Decision Logs (8x), verification notes (3x), micro-defences (3x), AI interaction cycle (weekly).
3. **Assessment weeks consolidate.** Weeks 4, 8, 12 introduce no new tools or concepts.
4. **Worked examples before independent performance.** Every new skill begins with instructor demonstration.
5. **Vocabulary pre-training.** 3-5 terms introduced before (not during) each session's main activity.
6. **Pair work every week.** Interaction is a condition for language acquisition.
7. **Deliberate mismatch.** AI tasks are designed so output sometimes doesn't match intent, requiring genuine meaning negotiation.
8. **Git is infrastructure.** Introduced in Week 3 as "how work gets saved and shared," not as a technology unit. AI handles commands.
9. **Language goals are primary.** The course teaches English and tool discovery. Artifacts are vehicles for language development, not the destination. The organizing question is "Can you say what you mean?" not "What are you going to build?"
10. **Pre-task planning.** Every Segment B begins with 5-10 minutes of planning time (reviewing the task, organizing ideas, consulting Goal Document) before students begin working. This increases complexity and fluency of output without reducing accuracy.
11. **Project types are informational.** Four common project types are presented in Week 4 so students know what people build with the college-provided tools. Students who want to go beyond these using tools they have access to are welcome to. This is context, not a scope constraint.
12. **Changing your mind is learning.** The Goal Document celebrates revision. Dropping a weak idea and trying something new is evidence of language development (the student can now articulate why the old idea didn't work and what they want instead). Fail-fast is built into the design: discovering that your words don't work and revising them IS the learning.
13. **AI expands imagination.** Students use AI not just to execute existing ideas but to discover possibilities they hadn't imagined (Mollick-style creativity). This is especially important in Week 2, where students who don't have strong wishes can ask AI to help them think bigger.

## Project Types (Presented Week 4)

Common project types are presented in Week 4 as examples of what people build. This is not a decision point. Students are told "You don't need to choose anything. This is just so you know what's possible." Over the following weeks, students see worked examples and gradually develop their own ideas.

| Type | Description | Baseline tools | Example |
|------|-------------|---------------|---------|
| **Document** | A structured, accessible document for a specific audience | Word + Copilot, Accessibility Checker | A 20-page illustrated handbook for newcomers to Canada, with navigation and professional layout |
| **Data tool** | A spreadsheet that organizes, calculates, and visualizes data | Excel + Copilot | An expense tracker that categorizes purchases, flags unusual spending, and generates monthly reports with charts |
| **Presentation** | A slide deck with speaker notes for a specific purpose | PowerPoint + Copilot, Accessibility Checker | A pitch deck for a small business idea, with data visualizations and a consistent visual identity |
| **Game** | An interactive game or simulation | AI + browser | An escape-room puzzle game playable in a browser, with clues, a timer, and a scoring system |
| **Code port** | Porting an existing open-source project to a new language or platform | AI coding agent + Git | Porting a classic game (e.g., SimCity 1989) from C to TypeScript so it runs in a browser, using property-based testing to verify the AI's output matches the original |
| **Video** | A video or animation for a specific audience | Clipchamp, phone camera | A mini-documentary about your neighbourhood, with narration, captions, and AI-generated transitions |
| **Art** | Digital art, illustration, or design | AI image generation, design tools | A series of AI-generated illustrations for a personal calendar, each in a consistent artistic style |
| **Novel / creative writing** | A sustained piece of narrative or creative writing | Word + Copilot | A complete illustrated short story with chapter headings, character portraits, and a designed cover |
| **Combination** | A multi-part project using 2+ types | Any combination | A personal portfolio website with an embedded project gallery, resume, and contact form |

These types reflect what's possible with the college-provided baseline (Office 365 + Copilot). Students who want to go further using tools they have access to are welcome to. The assessment is on articulation, documentation, and communication, not on which tools were used.

## Session Structure (3 x 55-Minute Periods)

| Segment | Duration | Purpose |
|---------|----------|---------|
| **A: Vocabulary + Worked Example** | ~40 min | Pre-train terms. Instructor demonstrates the week's challenge as a worked example. |
| **B: Planning + Guided Practice** | ~55 min | **5-10 min pre-task planning** (students review the task, consult Goal Document, organize approach). Then: students work on the challenge with support. Pair work. Decision Log or verification note. |
| *Break* | ~15 min | |
| **C: Application + Goal Document** | ~55 min | Apply the skill to own content. Goal Document writing and pair discussion. Whole-class debrief. |

Total: 150 min instruction + 15 min break = 165 min (3 x 55).

Segment B's planning phase is not setup time. Students use it to review the task, think about what they want to accomplish, and connect it to their Goal Document. For micro-defence weeks, planning time is especially valuable: students review their portfolio, choose what to present, and organize their explanation.

Segment C's Goal Document time is not optional filler. It is where students practise the most important language in the course: articulating what they want, evaluating what happened, describing possibilities, and reflecting on how their thinking has changed. The question driving Segment C is "Can you say what you mean?" not "What are you going to build?"

## Phase 1: Discovery — "What's possible?" (Weeks 1-4)

Students arrive not knowing what they can do with a computer beyond the apps they already use. Phase 1 blows open those assumptions and develops the language skills students need to articulate what they want.

The Goal Document arc across Phase 1 is about richer articulation, not project convergence: wanting (Week 1) → evaluating (Week 2) → speculating (Week 3) → reflecting (Week 4). Some students will have consistent interests across four weeks; others will change direction completely. Both are good. Changing your mind after trying things is what learning looks like.

**Goal Document complexity level: Listing and describing.** Students write simple sentences expressing preferences and describing concrete things. No justification or evaluation required yet.

### Week 1: What Can a Computer Do for Me?

**Challenge:** Most students think of computers as places where apps live. This week reframes: a computer is a workspace you control, and your files are yours.

**New vocabulary (5):** folder, file path, file extension, cloud sync, version

**Segment A -- Worked example + possibilities showcase:** Instructor shows 3-4 examples of things real people have built by describing what they wanted to AI: a working web app that tracks study habits and shows weekly patterns on a phone, a children's picture book with AI-generated illustrations, an interactive quiz game playable in a browser with scoring and a leaderboard, a personal portfolio website with project gallery and contact form. "All of these were built by people who don't write code. They told AI what they wanted." Then: instructor shows their own file system. Where things live. How they're organized.

**Segment B -- Planning + guided practice:** Planning (5 min): "Before you open your computer, think: where are your files right now? If someone asked you to find a file from last semester, could you?" Students explore their own OneDrive. Map current file system on paper. In pairs, compare: "Where are your files? Can you find them?" Create a course folder structure using a template.

**Segment C -- Goal Document launch:** Students start their Goal Document (a shared doc in their course folder). "Every week you'll write in a document called your Goal Document. It's where you practise saying what you want, what you're interested in, and how your thinking is changing. You're not committing to anything. You're practising the skill of figuring out what you want and putting it into words." Prompt: *"Think about your life, your studies, or your work. Write 3-5 things you wish your computer could do for you. These don't have to be realistic. Write what you actually want."*

Sentence frames:
- "I wish my computer could _____."
- "It would help me if I could use a computer to _____."
- "I want to learn how to _____."

Pairs share and discuss: "What did you write? Why?"

**CLOs:** 1 (begin)

---

### Week 2: Getting AI to Do What I Mean

**Challenge:** You have ideas. AI can execute them. But can you say what you mean clearly enough for it to work?

**New vocabulary (5):** prompt, generate, revise, accept/reject, verification

**Segment A -- Worked example:** Instructor picks a goal from their own wish list: "I want a one-page summary of this article for my students." Uses Copilot. First attempt doesn't quite work (deliberate mismatch). Instructor revises the prompt, evaluates again, accepts with changes. Completes a Decision Log entry on the projector, thinking aloud.

**Segment B -- Planning + guided practice:** Planning (5 min): students review their wish list from Week 1. "Pick one that interests you. OR: if none of them excite you, that's fine. You can ask AI for ideas." Two practice paths, both valid:

- **Path A (most students):** Try to get Copilot to help with a wish from their list.
- **Path B (students who want to explore):** Ask Copilot to suggest possibilities. "I'm interested in _____. What could I make with a computer that would help me?" Then try one suggestion. (This is Mollick-style discovery: using AI to expand what you can imagine.)

Students try their approach. After ~10 min: "Stop. Look at what you got. Is it what you wanted? If not, where's the mismatch?" Revise and try again. Complete first Decision Log entry as a class. In pairs: "What did you ask for? What did you get? What would you change?"

**Segment C -- Goal Document update:** *"Which of your wishes could you explain most clearly? Which ones got clearer when you tried them? Did you discover any new ideas today? Write about how your thinking has changed."*

Sentence frames:
- "I think AI could help me _____ because _____."
- "My most interesting idea right now is _____ because _____."
- "I changed my mind about _____ because _____."
- "Today I discovered that _____, which I didn't know before."

Pairs discuss: "What's the most interesting thing you tried or discovered today? Did your thinking change?"

**CLOs:** 3 (begin), 4 (begin)
**Assessment:** Decision Log entries begin

**DL/GD distinction this week:** The Decision Log records what AI did and what the student decided (evaluative, backward-looking). The Goal Document records which ideas interest the student now and how their thinking has changed (exploratory, forward-looking). Both are assigned; they prompt different cognitive operations.

---

### Week 3: Never Losing Work Again

**Challenge:** You've started building things. What happens when you delete something by accident? When you want to go back to yesterday's version? When you want to share your work?

**New vocabulary (5):** repository, commit, snapshot, restore, push

**Segment A -- Visual pre-training + worked example:** Before any tool: a visual model of version control as a timeline of snapshots. "You already have version history in OneDrive. Git is the same idea, but you control when saves happen, and it works for sharing." Worked example: instructor creates a repository, commits a file, changes it, commits again, restores the first version. All through Copilot: "Save this version," "Show me what I had before," "Go back."

**Segment B -- Planning + guided practice:** Planning (5 min): "Think about a time you lost work or couldn't find an old version of something. What would you want to save and protect?" Students create a portfolio repository. Add Week 1 file map and Week 2 document. First commits with Copilot. In pairs: one changes a file, partner guides the commit. Then: restore practice. "Change something, save it, then get the old version back."

**Segment C -- Goal Document update:** "You've been writing about what you want for two weeks. Today: try to describe something that doesn't exist yet. This is harder. You have to use words like 'it might be' and 'it would look like' and 'I imagine.' This is the kind of language that lets you plan things before you make them." Prompt: *"Think about the things you've been interested in. If you could make something with a computer, what kind of thing might it be? A document? A spreadsheet? A presentation? Something else? Describe what someone would see if they looked at it."*

Sentence frames:
- "I'm curious about making a _____ because _____."
- "It might look like _____, with _____."
- "I'm not sure yet, but I imagine something that _____."

Pairs share: "Can you picture it? What would someone see? What would it do?"

**CLOs:** 1

---

### Week 4: Where Am I Now?

**Challenge:** Take stock. What have you learned? What interests you? Can you explain your work to someone else?

**No new vocabulary.** Review of all 15 terms from Weeks 1-3.

**Project types presented this week (informational only).** Instructor presents four project types (Document, Data Tool, Presentation, Combination) with brief descriptions and one example per type. "You don't need to choose anything. This is just so you know what's possible." Students are not asked to commit.

**Segment A:** Students prepare file-system artefact for submission. Vocabulary review (Weeks 1-3). Project type presentation: "What Do People Build With These Tools?" -- informational, not a decision point.

**Segment B:** Planning (5-10 min): students review their portfolio and Goal Document, preparing for micro-defence. In-class written diagnostic (10 min, unmediated): *"Describe something you tried to do with AI this semester. What happened? What would you do differently?"* Oral Micro-Defence 1 while others work.

**Segment C:** Structured peer response on Goal Documents (pairs read each other's GD, all entries Weeks 1-3, and answer: "What seems to interest your partner most? What's unclear or what would you want to know more about?"). Goal Document reflection: *"What have you learned in 4 weeks? What can you do now that you couldn't before? What interests you most right now? Has your thinking changed since Week 1?"*

Sentence frames:
- "The most interesting thing I've learned is _____."
- "I want to try _____ next because _____."
- "My thinking changed about _____ because _____."
- "I can now _____, which I couldn't do before."

**Instructor reads Goal Documents after this week and returns brief comments (1-3 sentences) on clarity of thinking and language development.** Comment on whether the student's thinking is developing and whether you can tell what interests them. Comment on clarity of language, not on whether they've chosen a project.

**Assessment:**
- File System artifact package due (7%)
- Oral Micro-Defence 1 (incl. diagnostic + peer feedback)
- CLOs 2, 5: **diagnostic only**

## Phase 2: Building — "What do I need to learn?" (Weeks 5-10)

Students now have a sense of what's possible and what interests them. Phase 2 builds specific capabilities while students continue developing their thinking. Each week's challenge connects to the question: "What can I do with this? How does it connect to what I'm interested in?"

**Goal Document complexity level: Planning and justifying.** Students write about feasibility, conditions, and reasons. Conditional language ("If I want to..., I would need to..."), technical vocabulary, and reasoning about constraints.

### Week 5: Making Something Others Can Read and Use

**Challenge:** You can make a document. But can someone else actually use it? Can a screen reader read it? Is it structured or just formatted to look pretty?

**New vocabulary (5):** heading (style), alt text, screen reader, Accessibility Checker, contrast

**Segment A -- Worked example:** Two versions of the same document: one "looks nice" (bold headings, no structure), one is properly structured (styles, alt text). Instructor runs Accessibility Checker. "This one looks good but is broken for anyone using assistive technology." Fixes issues with Copilot.

**Segment B -- Planning + guided practice:** Planning (5 min): "Think about who would read a document you make. What might make it hard for them?" Students take a poorly formatted document and make it accessible. Run Accessibility Checker. Write verification note in own words: what AI did, what they changed, what they checked.

**Segment C -- Goal Document + application:** Students apply accessibility principles to a document related to their own project goal. *"Think about who will use what you build. What would make it hard for them to use? What accessibility issues should you plan for?"*

Sentence frames:
- "The person using my project might have trouble with _____ because _____."
- "I need to check _____ to make sure it's accessible."
- "One thing I learned today that applies to my project is _____."

Pairs review each other's work.

**CLOs:** 6 (begin), 4
**Assessment:** Decision Log feedback round 1 returned; Git Restore episode opportunity

---

### Week 6: Making Sense of Numbers

**Challenge:** You have data (a survey, a grade list, a budget, a schedule). Can you understand what it says? Can you get AI to help you show it to someone else?

**New vocabulary (5):** cell, row, column, formula, chart

**Segment A -- Worked example (reading first, then building):** Instructor presents a finished spreadsheet someone else made (class survey results with formulas and a chart). "Before we build anything, let's read this. What does this formula do? What does this chart show? Is this number right?" Students practise interpreting an existing spreadsheet. Then: instructor builds a new summary using Copilot. Demonstrates the "explain this" check: "Copilot, what does this formula do?" Verifies one calculation manually.

**Segment B -- Planning + guided practice:** Planning (5 min): "What data do you encounter in your life? A budget? Grades? A work schedule? What questions would you want answered?" Students work with a provided dataset. First: answer 3 questions about an existing spreadsheet (reading comprehension). Then: create a summary and chart with Copilot. Verify one formula. Decision Log entry.

**Segment C -- Goal Document:** *"Think about your project. If it involves numbers or data, what would you need to organize and show? If it doesn't, what data from your life would be useful to have in a spreadsheet?"*

Sentence frames:
- "My project involves data about _____. I would want to show _____."
- "A spreadsheet would help me _____ because _____."
- "I don't need data for my project, but I could use a spreadsheet to _____."

Pairs work with each other's data ideas.

**CLOs:** 3, 4, 6

---

### Week 7: Working Together Without Breaking Things

**Challenge:** You want to work with someone else on a project. How do you contribute to the same thing without stepping on each other's work?

**New vocabulary (5):** pull request (PR), issue, fork, comment, review

**Segment A -- Worked example:** Instructor demonstrates PRs and Issues as communication genres: "A PR says 'here's what I changed and why.' An Issue says 'here's a problem or idea.'" Full demo: fork, change, PR with description, comment exchange. Good vs. bad examples.

**Segment B -- Planning + guided practice:** Planning (5 min): "Think about a time you worked on something with another person. What went wrong? How did you divide the work?" Pairs fork a shared repository, make changes, submit PRs with descriptions, comment on partner's work.

**Segment C -- Goal Document:** *"Think about your project. What part is hardest? What part could someone else help with? Write a GitHub Issue describing one task on your project that a partner could pick up."*

Sentence frames:
- "I need help with _____. The task is to _____."
- "To do this task, you would need to know _____."
- "The result should look like _____."

Even if the project is individual, this practises task articulation and scoping.

**CLOs:** 1, 5, 7 (begin)

---

### Week 8: When Things Go Wrong

**Challenge:** Something broke. The formula gives the wrong answer. The commit failed. Copilot misunderstood you. Now what?

**New vocabulary (5):** error message, troubleshoot, debug, help request, log

**Segment A -- Troubleshooting worked examples:** Instructor presents 4 real error scenarios, including at least one Git failure (e.g., AI committed the wrong file, a merge went sideways after a PR):
1. A Word document where Copilot reformatted everything incorrectly
2. An Excel formula returning an error
3. A Git commit that included the wrong file (recovery via restore)
4. An error message from a failed operation

For each: parse the error (what happened, where, what to try). Then: demonstrate writing a help request (context → problem → what I tried → what I expect). "A good help request is the most useful thing you can write in a professional setting."

**Segment B -- Planning + micro-defences:** Planning (5-10 min for micro-defence preparation): students review portfolio and choose what to present. In-class written diagnostic (10 min). Oral Micro-Defence 2 while others practise troubleshooting staged errors and writing help requests.

**Structured peer response on Goal Documents** (pairs read each other's GD and answer: "Is the project plan clear enough that you could help with it? What questions do you have?").

**Segment C -- Goal Document:** *"Today was about things going wrong. Think about your project: what's the riskiest part? What's most likely to not work? Write a help request for a problem you think might happen."*

Sentence frames:
- "The riskiest part of my project is _____ because _____."
- "If _____ goes wrong, I would try _____."
- "I need help with _____. I tried _____, but _____. I expected _____."

Pairs exchange help requests and try to answer each other's.

**Instructor reads Goal Documents after this week and returns brief comments (1-3 sentences) on clarity, feasibility, and progress since Week 4.**

**Assessment:** Oral Micro-Defence 2 (incl. diagnostic + peer feedback)
**CLOs assessed:** 2, 5

**DL/GD distinction this week:** The Decision Log (if written this week) records a specific AI interaction and the student's evaluation of it. The Goal Document records anticipated risks and how to handle them. If a student writes both, the DL is backward-looking (what happened) and the GD is forward-looking (what might happen).

### Week 9: Building My Thing (Part 1)

**Challenge:** You've been refining your goal for 8 weeks. You have the skills. Start building.

**New vocabulary (5):** slide, layout, speaker notes, caption, colour contrast
*(Introduced because some students' projects involve presentations. Others may not need these terms this week, but all benefit from the vocabulary for collaborative work later.)*

**Segment A -- Self-selected project-type demonstrations:** Students group by project type. Each group watches a short worked example relevant to their type (~10 min). Possible groupings:
- **Document / novel group:** Pulling together structured or narrative content with Copilot + Accessibility Checker
- **Data tool group:** Going further with Excel, including multi-sheet and charting
- **Presentation / video group:** Copilot in PowerPoint + speaker notes; Clipchamp basics
- **Game group:** Using AI to generate and test interactive content in a browser
- **Art group:** AI image generation + design layout
- **Combination group:** Linking outputs across tools (e.g., data visualization embedded in a report)

Instructor groups these flexibly based on what students are actually building (some groups may combine if small). After demonstrations: gallery walk. Each group posts a 1-sentence summary of what they learned. Students circulate and read other groups' summaries.

**Segment B -- Planning + project work:** Planning (10 min): students open their Goal Document, review their project plan, and write a concrete task list for today. "What specifically will you build in the next hour?" Students build. Instructor circulates. This is not free time; students should be working toward a specific artefact they defined in their Goal Document.

**Segment C -- Peer check-in:** Pairs show each other what they're building. "Does this look like what you described in your Goal Document? What's missing?" Decision Log entry on the project work.

**CLOs:** 3, 4, 6

---

### Week 10: Building My Thing (Part 2) + Submission

**Challenge:** Finish, check, and submit your mid-semester artefact. Does it do what you wanted?

**No new vocabulary.** Consolidation.

**Segment A -- Verification and quality check:** Instructor models the final review process: run Accessibility Checker, write verification note, re-read Goal Document — "Did I build what I said I would?" Shows a worked example of the gap between intention and result and how to write about it honestly.

**Segment B -- Planning + finalisation:** Planning (5 min): students re-read their Goal Document and check their artefact against their stated plan. Students complete and submit artefact with verification note. Commit to portfolio.

**Segment C -- Goal Document reflection + Creation phase preview:** *"Compare what you planned with what you built. Write 3-4 sentences about the gap."*

Sentence frames:
- "I set out to build _____. What I actually built is _____."
- "The biggest difference is _____ because _____."
- "If I had more time, I would change _____."
- "I learned that _____ is harder/easier than I expected because _____."

Then: introduction to the collaborative project. Form pairs/groups based on shared interests or complementary skills. Begin discussing: "What do *we* want to build together?"

**Assessment:** Mid-Semester Artifact Package due (8%); Decision Log feedback round 2 returned

## Phase 3: Creation — "Does it do what I wanted?" (Weeks 11-14)

Students have built an individual artefact. Now they collaborate on something bigger. The goal thread shifts from "what do I want?" to "what do we want?" and finally to "did we build it?"

**Goal Document complexity level: Evaluating and comparing.** Students write about gaps between plans and results. Subordination ("Although I planned to..., I ended up..."), hedging ("This evidence is probably strong enough to show..."), evaluative language ("The most convincing part is..."), and comparison across time ("In Week 4 I thought..., but now...").

### Week 11: What Do We Want to Build Together?

**Challenge:** Agree on a shared goal. Divide the work so everyone contributes. Start building.

**No new vocabulary.**

**Collaborative roles:** Each group member takes an explicit role that rotates or persists across Weeks 11-13:
- **Coordinator:** Maintains the project plan (Issues), tracks what's done and what's next.
- **Builder:** Leads artefact production for the current session.
- **Reviewer:** Reviews all PRs, checks accessibility, writes verification notes.

Roles ensure all students produce language (the Reviewer must write, the Coordinator must plan, the Builder must describe). In groups of 2, combine Builder and one other role. In groups of 4, double the Builder role.

**Segment A -- Collaborative scoping:** Instructor models project scoping with roles using a sample project (e.g., "a campus survival app for new students: an interactive FAQ web page, a budget calculator, and a short orientation video"). The Coordinator writes the first Issue. The Builder identifies the first task. The Reviewer describes what "done" looks like for that task.

**Information-gap structure:** Each group member writes independently (3 min) what they think the project should be and what they can contribute. Then the group shares. The Coordinator synthesizes. Disagreements must be negotiated, not voted on. "If you can't explain why you want X, you can't just outvote someone who can explain why they want Y."

Groups define their project, create a shared repository, write Issues for the first tasks.

**Segment B -- Planning + project work:** Planning (5 min): each role reviews their responsibilities for the session. Groups build. Each student submits at least one PR with description and comments on a partner's work. (Collaboration episode for Git Episodes.)

**Segment C -- Status update:** Each group: 2-minute structured update using a template:
- "We are building _____."
- "[Coordinator] says: we have finished _____. Next we need to _____."
- "[Reviewer] says: the quality of _____ is _____. We need to improve _____."
- "Our biggest challenge is _____."

Class asks questions. Instructor provides feedback on project scope and feasibility.

Goal Document (individual, 10 min): *"What is your role in the group? What are you responsible for this week?"*

**CLOs:** 1, 5, 7

---

### Week 12: Is It Working?

**Challenge:** Check your progress against your plan. What's working? What isn't? Can you explain it?

**Roles continue.** Coordinator updates Issues. Reviewer checks current artefact quality. Builder continues production.

**Segment A -- Portfolio curation preview:** Instructor demonstrates mapping evidence to CLOs. Worked example of an Evidence-Indexed Reflection entry. Students identify strongest evidence for 2-3 CLOs.

**Segment B -- Planning + micro-defences + project work:** Planning (5-10 min for micro-defence preparation): students review their full portfolio across 12 weeks, choose what to present, and organize their explanation. In-class written diagnostic (10 min). Oral Micro-Defence 3 while others continue projects.

**Structured peer response on Goal Documents** (pairs from different groups read each other's GD and answer: "Is the progression from Week 1 to now clear? What's the strongest change in thinking?").

**Segment C -- Goal Document (collaborative):** Groups write a joint progress report. *"What did we set out to build? What's done? What's left? What's the biggest problem we've solved?"*

Then individual Goal Document entry (10 min): *"Compare what you wrote in Week 4 with where you are now. What changed? Why?"*

Sentence frames:
- "In Week 4, I planned to _____. Now I am _____."
- "The biggest change is _____ because _____."
- "I didn't expect _____, but it turned out that _____."

**Assessment:** Oral Micro-Defence 3 (incl. diagnostic + peer feedback)
**CLOs assessed:** 2, 5

---

### Week 13: Making It Better

**Challenge:** Iterate. What would you change? What feedback did you get? Is your evidence strong?

**Roles continue.** Final push on collaborative artefact.

**Accountability mechanism:** Each group member submits an individual "contribution statement" (2-3 sentences) describing what they built, what PRs they submitted, and what they reviewed. This is checked against the Git history. It is not graded separately, but the instructor uses it to verify that all members contributed meaningfully.

**Segment A -- Self-assessment calibration:** Instructor returns Decision Log self-assessment patterns. Worked example of full Evidence-Indexed Reflection including evaluative judgment ("How strong is this evidence?", "What would I still need to work on?").

**Segment B -- Portfolio curation + project refinement:** Students map evidence to CLOs and draft reflection. Partners from different groups: "Is this evidence convincing? What's missing?" Project iteration based on Week 12 feedback. Collaborative artefact due end of session.

**Segment C -- Final Goal Document entry + Decision Logs due.**

Goal Document prompt: *"Read your Goal Document from the beginning. Write a final entry that answers: What did I learn about what I want? How did my goals change? What can I do now that I couldn't do in Week 1?"*

Sentence frames:
- "When I started, I thought _____. Now I think _____."
- "The most important thing I learned is _____ because _____."
- "My goals changed from _____ to _____ because _____."
- "I can now _____, which I couldn't do before because _____."

**Instructor reads Goal Documents after this week and returns brief final comments.**

**CLOs:** All
**Assessment:** Decision Logs due (8 entries, 30%)

**Challenge:** The question the whole course has been building toward.

## The Goal Document

The Goal Document is a shared doc (in the student's portfolio repository) that develops across the semester. It is not graded, but the instructor reads it at **Weeks 4, 8, and 13** and provides brief written comments (1-3 sentences on clarity of thinking and language development). Structured peer response occurs at **Weeks 4, 8, and 12**.

### Complexity grading

The Goal Document is designed so that the cognitive and linguistic demands increase across the semester, even though the genre (goal articulation + reflection) stays constant.

| Weeks | Complexity demands | Language features expected |
|-------|-------------------|--------------------------|
| 1-4 | **Listing and describing.** Name preferences, describe concrete things, choose among options. Few elements to manage. | Simple sentences, "I want...", "I think...", concrete nouns, personal vocabulary |
| 5-8 | **Planning and justifying.** Reason about feasibility, anticipate problems, scope tasks, explain choices. Multiple elements and conditions. | Conditional ("If I want to..., I need to..."), causal ("because"), technical vocabulary, longer sentences |
| 9-14 | **Evaluating and comparing.** Compare intention with result, assess evidence strength, synthesize across time. Requires managing past and present simultaneously. | Subordination ("Although...", "Even though..."), hedging ("probably", "might"), evaluative ("The strongest evidence is..."), temporal comparison ("In Week 4... but now...") |

## Decision Log / Goal Document Distinction

Both are written reflections, but they prompt different cognitive operations:

| | Decision Log | Goal Document |
|--|-------------|---------------|
| **Direction** | Backward-looking: what happened | Forward-looking: what I want, what I'm learning, how my thinking is changing |
| **Trigger** | A specific AI interaction | The week's challenge connected to the student's evolving interests |
| **Core question** | "What did AI do, and what did I decide?" | "Can I say what I mean? How has my thinking changed?" |
| **Frequency** | 8 of 12 eligible weeks (student chooses) | Every week (brief, 10-15 min) |
| **Graded** | Yes (30%) | No (instructor reads at Weeks 4, 8, 13) |

Because students choose which 8 weeks to write Decision Log entries, overlap with Goal Document is intermittent and manageable. Where both occur in the same week, the prompts target different operations: the DL evaluates a past interaction; the GD develops the student's ability to articulate what interests them and how their thinking is changing.

## Fluency Development

| Strand | Where | Recurrence |
|--------|-------|------------|
| **Meaning-focused input** | AI output, error messages, technical instructions, peer work, reading existing spreadsheets | Every week |
| **Meaning-focused output** | Decision Logs, verification notes, help requests, PR descriptions, Goal Document entries | Every week |
| **Language-focused learning** | Vocabulary pre-training; sentence frames; genre templates | Every week |
| **Fluency development** | Same task types across 14 weeks: Decision Logs (8x), micro-defences (3x), verification notes (3x), Goal Document entries (13x), AI interaction cycle (weekly) | Built in |

The Goal Document is itself a fluency builder: students write about what they want using increasingly specific language across 13 entries. The genre is always the same (goal articulation + reflection), but the content develops and the linguistic demands increase from listing and describing (Weeks 1-4) through planning and justifying (Weeks 5-8) to evaluating and comparing (Weeks 9-14). The Phase 1 arc (wanting → evaluating → speculating → reflecting) develops richer articulation, not project convergence; changing your mind is evidence of language growth.
