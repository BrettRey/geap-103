# GEAP 103 Week 14: Instructor Materials Package

**Title:** Did I Build What I Wanted?
**Phase:** Creation (Week 14 of 14 -- final week)
**Contact time:** 3 x 55 min (165 min total: 150 min instruction + 15 min break)
**Date prepared:** 2026-03-28

This file contains everything you need to prepare slides and run the class for Week 14. The lesson plan (`lesson-plans/week-14.md`) has the pedagogical rationale. This file has the concrete content. In case of conflict between this materials package and the lesson plan for Week 14, this materials package governs (it reflects March 2026 design revisions). For anything not covered here, the lesson plan governs.

**Week 14 design note:** This is the final class. One graded item is due: the Final Artifact Package (10%). Everything else is celebratory. The session review (explaining to a future student) is the last of 14 weeks of AI-assisted speaking practice. The sharing event (individual and group presentations) is ungraded. The wrap-up connects course skills to degree programs. The course ends with the student's voice, not their writing.

**No new vocabulary.** All 40 terms from Weeks 1--9 should be part of active vocabulary. The best evidence of acquisition is this week: students use terms without prompting across presentations, discussions, and reflections.

**No written Goal Document entry this week.** The oral sharing answers the Goal Document prompt ("Is this what I set out to make? What changed?"). The shift from written to oral is deliberate.

**AI tools:** Same tool-agnostic approach. AI provides the interlocutor role during the session review. No AI during the sharing event or discussions.

## 2. Segment A: Final Submissions + Session Review (0:00--0:40)

### Framing (0:00--0:05)

**Format:** Whole class

> "This is the last class. One thing is due today: your Final Artifact Package. Then we share."

Display the submission checklist:

> **Final Artifact Package -- what to submit:**
>
> | Component | What it is |
> |-----------|-----------|
> | **The artifact itself** | The thing you built |
> | **Process documentation** | In your own words: what AI did, what you changed, what you checked |
> | **Verification note** | What was checked, what was found, what was not completed |
> | **Evidence of sharing** | Peer check-ins, session review transcripts, collaborative work evidence |

> "If your artifact isn't perfect, that's fine. The documentation matters as much as the product. If your verification note honestly names what you didn't finish, that's better than pretending everything is done."

**Deadline:** Submit by 0:40. Late submissions accepted until 11:59 PM tonight.

---

### Finalisation and Submission (0:05--0:25)

**Format:** Individual work with instructor circulation

Students finalise and submit. Tasks:

1. **Complete the artifact** if needed
2. **Write or revise the verification note:** What was checked, what was found, what was not completed
3. **Ensure process documentation** is in their own words (use the process table from Week 9)
4. **Compile evidence of sharing** (peer check-ins, session review transcripts, collaborative work)

**What to say while circulating:**

| What you see | What to say |
|-------------|------------|
| Student unsure what to include | "Four things: the artifact, your process documentation, your verification note, and your sharing evidence. Check the slide." |
| Student anxious about completeness | "Submit what you have. Honest documentation is the skill. A verification note that says 'I didn't finish X' is better than no note." |
| Student with suspiciously polished documentation | "Can you read me your verification note? Does it sound like you?" |
| Student missing sharing evidence | "What's in your sharing evidence? The peer check-in from Week 9? The session review transcript? A screenshot of your group PR?" |
| Student finished early | "Good. Double-check that everything uploaded correctly. Then think about what you'll say during the sharing." |

### Bridging Activity (0:25--0:27)

**Format:** Individual, silent

> "Before the sharing event, one last conversation with AI. Think about a new student who will take this course next semester. What would you tell them? What do they need to know?"

---

### Session Review (0:27--0:38)

**Format:** Individual, AI voice mode

**Role-setting prompt** (on slide):

> Speaking practice. I'm a B1 English student practising explaining my work. Ask me follow-up questions. If my explanation is vague, tell me what's unclear and ask me to be more specific. Don't do the explaining for me.

**This week's scenario** (on slide):

> Explain your final artifact to AI as if AI were a new student who will take this course next semester. What should they know about what you built and what you learned?

AI role: Future student.

Students talk to AI for 10 minutes. The AI asks follow-ups: "What was the hardest part?" "What would you do differently?" "What advice would you give me?"

This is the final session review. The scenario is reflective and forward-looking, a fitting end to 14 weeks of speaking practice.

**Self-check** (1 min):

> "Write two sentences: What did the 'future student' ask about? What advice did you give?"

**Note:** Voice mode starts a new chat.

---

### Final Submissions (0:38--0:40)

**Format:** Individual

> "Last call for submissions. If you haven't submitted, submit now. The late deadline is 11:59 PM tonight, but submit now if you can."

Students who haven't submitted: submit now.

## 3. Segment B: Sharing Event (0:40--1:35)

### Sharing Setup (0:40--0:45)

**Format:** Whole class

> "Now we share. This isn't graded. It's a celebration. But even celebrations benefit from a moment of planning."
>
> "Two rounds: first, individual sharing -- you show what you built. Then, group presentations -- your group shows what you built together."

---

### Individual Sharing (0:45--1:10)

**Format:** Whole class, informal presentations (2--3 min each)

Students share informally. No slides required. Show the artifact on screen, say what you learned, answer one question.

**Structure per student (~2--3 min):**

1. **Show what you built** (30--60 sec)
2. **Say what you learned** (30--60 sec)
3. **"Is this what you set out to make? What changed?"** (30--60 sec)

Instructor and classmates ask one brief follow-up question.

**Sentence frames** *(optional -- on slide only for students who want them)*:

> - "I built _____ because _____."
> - "The hardest part was _____."
> - "I planned to _____, but what actually happened is _____."
> - "If I started again, I would _____."

**Instructor note:** These frames are available but should not be pushed. By Week 14, most students should be able to present without scaffolding. The frames are there for the student who freezes, not as a default.

**Pacing:**

| Class size | Time at 2 min each | Time at 3 min each | Fit in 25 min? |
|-----------|-------------------|-------------------|----------------|
| 12 | 24 min | 36 min | Yes at 2 min |
| 16 | 32 min | 48 min | No -- split or overflow |
| 20 | 40 min | 60 min | No -- split or overflow |

**For large classes:** Split into two groups presenting simultaneously in different areas of the room. Or: do half in Segment B, half at the start of Segment C.

**Tone:** Celebratory. Do not grade. Listen, react, ask genuine questions. "I didn't finish what I planned, but I learned a lot" is a perfect answer.

---

### Collaborative Group Presentations (1:10--1:35)

**Format:** Group presentations (3--5 min per group)

Each group presents. Roles are visible:

| Role | What they do |
|------|-------------|
| **Coordinator** | Introduces: what they set out to build, how they divided work |
| **Builder** | Demonstrates: shows the artifact, points out key features, describes what AI did vs. what the group did |
| **Reviewer** | Evaluates: what worked, what didn't, what they'd change |

**Structure per group (~3--5 min):**

1. Coordinator introduction (1 min)
2. Builder demonstration (1--2 min)
3. Reviewer evaluation (1 min)
4. One question from the class (1 min)

**Pacing:**

| Number of groups | Time at 3 min each | Time at 5 min each |
|-----------------|-------------------|-------------------|
| 5 | 15 min | 25 min |
| 7 | 21 min | 35 min |
| 8 | 24 min | 40 min |

Adjust pace to fit. If groups run long, cap at 4 min and hold questions.

**Tone:** Same as individual sharing. Celebratory. Ask the class: "What's one thing this group did that you hadn't thought of?"

## 5. Segment C: Wrap-Up (1:50--2:45)

### Overflow Presentations (1:50--2:00)

If needed, complete any remaining individual or group presentations. If all presentations are done, skip to "What Can You Do Now?"

---

### "What Can You Do Now That You Couldn't in Week 1?" (2:00--2:15)

**Format:** Pair discussion, then whole class

> "Not about your artifact. Not about your grade. About you. What can you do now that you couldn't do in Week 1?"

**Pair discussion with A/B turns** (5 min):

> "Tell your partner one specific thing you can do now. Not something big and abstract -- something specific."

**Whole-class sharing** (5 min): Volunteers share. Instructor writes responses on the board (or shared document).

**What to say while circulating during pair discussion:**

| What you hear | What to say |
|--------------|------------|
| "I learned a lot" (vague) | "What's one specific thing? Can you give an example?" |
| "I can use AI now" (too broad) | "What can you ask AI to do that you couldn't ask before?" |
| A specific, concrete skill | "That's a good example. Tell the class that one." |

**Instructor note:** Students are producing evaluative, comparative language ("I can now..., which I couldn't...") orally, without sentence frames, in a low-stakes setting. If the course worked, they can do this.

---

### Transfer to Degree Programs (2:15--2:25)

**Format:** Instructor-led

> "You're moving into your degree programs. The tools might change. The skills don't."

Display the transfer table:

> | What you learned here | Where it shows up next |
> |----------------------|----------------------|
> | Telling AI what you want | Every course that allows AI tools |
> | Evaluating whether output is right | Research, assignments, professional work |
> | Writing help requests and documentation | Group projects, IT support, workplace |
> | Working on shared projects with version control | Collaborative assignments, co-op |
> | Explaining your work to others | Presentations, interviews, meetings |
> | Organising files and versions | Every course, every job |

> "The one thing I hope you take from this course is that English is the control layer. If you can say what you mean clearly, you can get a computer to help you do almost anything."

---

### Course Evaluation (2:25--2:35)

**Format:** Individual

Students complete the institutional course evaluation.

> "I'm going to step away for 10 minutes so you can fill out the course evaluation honestly. Take your time. When you're done, close your laptop."

Instructor steps away from the room (or moves to a far corner out of sight) while students complete the evaluation. Return when most students have closed their laptops or after 10 minutes.

---

### Closing (2:35--2:45)

**Format:** Whole class

> "You started this course 14 weeks ago. Some of you didn't know what a file path was. Some of you had never used AI. All of you have now built something real, documented how you built it, shared it with others, and explained it in English."
>
> "Your Goal Documents tell the story of how your thinking changed -- 13 entries, from 'I wish my computer could...' to 'When I started I thought... now I think...' I've enjoyed reading them."
>
> "Good luck in your degree programs. You're ready."
