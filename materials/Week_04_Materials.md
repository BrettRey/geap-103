# GEAP 103 Week 4: Instructor Materials Package

**Title:** Where Am I Now?
**Phase:** Discovery (Week 4 of 14, consolidation)
**Contact time:** 3 × 55 min (165 min total: 150 min instruction + 15 min break)
**Date prepared:** 2026-03-25

This file contains everything you need to prepare slides and run the class for Week 4. The lesson plan (`GEAP_103_Week_4_Lesson_Plan_2026-02-12.md`) has the pedagogical rationale. This file has the concrete content. In case of conflict between this materials package and the lesson plan for Week 4, this materials package governs (it reflects March 2026 design revisions). For anything not covered here, the lesson plan governs.

**Week 4 design note:** This is the first assessment week. Two things are graded: the file-system artifact package (7%) and the first oral micro-defence (part of 25%). The rest of the session is review, peer response on Goal Documents, and a project-type menu that is purely informational. The most important thing today is that students experience explaining their work aloud for the first time, and that the experience is low-stakes enough to be useful rather than terrifying. The diagnostic writing (10 min, no AI) gives you an unmediated baseline for each student's independent written English.

**Format change from lesson plan:** The lesson plan describes individual micro-defences (one student presents to the instructor while others wait). This materials package uses **simultaneous triad defences** instead: groups of three, all presenting at the same time, instructor circulating. This gets all students through in one 30-minute block with no overflow into Week 5, and every student is speaking or giving feedback at all times. The instructor samples each group and asks one follow-up question per presenter.

**No new vocabulary.** All 15 terms from Weeks 1–3 are reviewed.

**AI tools:** Students do not use AI during the diagnostic (10 min). AI is available for all other activities. Same tool-agnostic approach as previous weeks.

## Before Class

### File-System Artifact Submission

Decide how students submit. Options:

| Method | Pros | Cons |
|--------|------|------|
| **OneDrive share link** | Students already have OneDrive; no new tool | You have to click through individual links |
| **Blackboard assignment** | Centralised; familiar to students | Students upload a screenshot or file list, not the live folder |
| **GitHub repository** | You can see the folder AND the commit history | Not all students will have pushed by Week 4 |
| **In-class spot-check** | You see the real thing on their device | Only works during class; no record |

**Recommendation:** OneDrive share link submitted via Blackboard, plus an in-class spot-check during Segment A. This gives you both the live folder and a submission record.

### Triad Groups

You need ~7 groups of 3 (for a class of 20–21; adjust for your class size). Options:

- **Pre-assigned:** Alphabetical, random draw, or mixed by comfort level. Recommended: mix stronger and weaker students so everyone hears a range of explanations.
- **Self-selected:** Faster setup, but friends cluster and you lose the diversity benefit.
- **Odd numbers:** If your class doesn't divide by 3, make one group of 4. The fourth student presents last; adjust rotation timing.

Have groups posted or ready to announce. Print one scoring sheet per student (template below).

### Diagnostic Setup

The 10-minute diagnostic is unmediated: no AI, no notes, no partner. Decide:

- **Handwritten:** Simplest. Hand out blank paper.
- **Locked-down device:** If your classroom supports it. Students close all browser tabs and apps, type in a plain document.

Handwritten is recommended. It removes any temptation to check AI and gives you a genuine writing sample.

## Segment A: Preparation + Review + Project Menu (0:00–0:40)

### Artifact Preparation (0:00–0:15)

Display:

> Before we start, take 10 minutes to make sure your geap-103 folder is ready:
>
> 1. Is it in your OneDrive?
> 2. Does every folder have a clear name? (lowercase-with-hyphens)
> 3. Are your files in the right folders? (Week 1 files in week-01, etc.)
> 4. Do you have a Goal Document with entries from Weeks 1–3?
> 5. If you have a GitHub repository, commit your current state now.
>
> When you're done, submit your OneDrive share link on Blackboard.

Circulate with quick spot-checks. For each student, glance at:

| What to check | What to say if it's missing |
|---------------|---------------------------|
| geap-103 folder exists in OneDrive | "Where are your course files? Let's find them and move them." |
| Subfolders exist (week-01 through week-14, or at least week-01 through week-03) | "Do you have folders for each week? You can create the rest now — ask AI if you want." |
| Files are in the right places | "This file is from Week 2 but it's in your top folder. Where should it go?" |
| Goal Document exists with content | "Where's your Goal Document? If you haven't started one, you'll write your first entry today." |
| Naming is consistent | "You have 'Week 1 stuff' and 'w2-files'. Pick one convention and use it everywhere. I recommend lowercase-with-hyphens." |

Students who finish early: help a neighbour, or review their GitHub repository for completeness.

**Students who are behind:** Some students will have missed a week, or their files are chaotic. That's fine. They participate in everything else today. They can submit the file-system artifact by end of Week 5 with no penalty — announce this now:

> "If your folder isn't ready yet, you have until the end of Week 5 to submit it. Focus on today's activities."

### Vocabulary Review (0:15–0:30)

All 15 terms from Weeks 1–3:

| Week 1 | Week 2 | Week 3 |
|--------|--------|--------|
| folder | prompt | repository |
| file path | generate | commit |
| file extension | revise | snapshot |
| cloud sync | accept / reject | restore |
| version | verification | push |

Display the full table. Run the review in two rounds:

**Round 1 — Instructor gives definition, students call out term (5 min):**

Work through all 15 in random order. Say the definition, students shout the term. Move fast. Examples:

- "What you type to tell AI what you want." → *prompt*
- "A folder with a memory." → *repository*
- "The letters after the dot that tell the computer the file type." → *file extension*
- "Putting your repository online." → *push*
- "Checking whether AI's output is correct." → *verification*

Skip any term the class gets instantly. Linger on terms that get silence.

**Round 2 — Pairs quiz each other (5 min):**

> "One partner covers the 'Meaning' column. The other points to a term. Define it. Then switch."

Circulate. Listen for accuracy. Note which terms are still shaky — you'll hear them during micro-defences later.

**Round 3 (optional, if time) — Instructor gives a scenario, students name the term:**

- "You made a change to your file and saved a new version to GitHub." → *commit*
- "You want to see what your file looked like last week." → *restore* (or *snapshot*)
- "AI made something, but it's wrong, so you try a new request." → *revise* (the prompt), *reject* (the output)

### What Do People Build With These Tools? (0:30–0:40)

This is informational only. No decisions, no commitments.

> "Over the next few weeks you'll learn more tools and discover more about what you can do. Here are some kinds of things people build. You don't need to choose anything today. This is just so you know what's possible."

Display one slide with the table. For each type, show one screenshot or 30-second example on the projector:

| Type | What it is | Example |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **Document** | Structured, accessible document for a specific audience | A 20-page illustrated handbook for newcomers to Canada, with navigation and professional layout |
| **Data Tool** | Spreadsheet that organises, calculates, and visualises data | An expense tracker that categorises purchases, flags unusual spending, and generates monthly reports with charts |
| **Presentation** | Slide deck with speaker notes for a specific purpose | A pitch deck for a small business idea, with data visualisations and a consistent visual identity |
| **Game** | An interactive game or simulation | An escape-room puzzle game playable in a browser, with clues, a timer, and a scoring system |
| **Video** | A video or animation for a specific audience | A mini-documentary about your neighbourhood, with narration, captions, and AI-generated transitions |
| **Art** | Digital art, illustration, or design | A series of AI-generated illustrations for a personal calendar, each in a consistent artistic style |
| **Novel** | A sustained piece of narrative or creative writing | A complete illustrated short story with chapter headings, character portraits, and a designed cover |
| **Combination** | A multi-part project using 2+ types | A personal portfolio website with an embedded project gallery, resume, and contact form |

> "These are examples, not limits. You can combine types, go beyond them, or invent something new. For now, just notice which ones are interesting to you."

Brief pair exchange (1 min): "Which type caught your eye? Tell your partner." No reporting out.

## Segment B: Diagnostic + Triad Defences (0:40–1:35)

### Pre-Task Planning (0:40–0:50)

Students prepare to explain their work. This is thinking time, not script-writing.

Display:

> In a few minutes, you'll explain one piece of work to two partners. Pick one:
>
> - Your file system (how you organised your folders and files)
> - A Decision Log entry (what you did with AI and what you decided)
> - Your GitHub repository (what you committed and why)
> - Something you made with AI (from Week 2 or 3)
>
> Open the thing you want to talk about. Think about:
>
> - What did you do?
> - What tools did you use?
> - What did you learn?
> - What would you do differently?
>
> Don't write a script. Just have it open and ready to show.

Circulate. Students who can't choose: "Which one are you most proud of? Start there." Students who have very little work: "Talk about the file system. Everyone has one."

---

### In-Class Written Diagnostic (0:50–1:00)

This is unmediated. No AI, no notes, no partner.

Display:

> **Writing task (10 minutes, no AI)**
>
> Describe something you tried to do with AI this semester. What happened? What would you do differently?
>
> Write on the paper provided. ~50–100 words.

Say:

> "Close your laptops. Close Copilot, ChatGPT, everything. This is just you and a piece of paper. I want to see what you can write on your own. It doesn't need to be perfect. Tell me what happened."

Hand out blank paper (or direct students to a locked-down document). Start a visible 10-minute timer.

**What you're looking for:** This is a baseline, not a test. You're establishing each student's unmediated written level so you can compare it against their AI-assisted work. Note:

| What you see | What it tells you |
|-------------|------------------|
| Clear story with specific details | Student can narrate a technical experience independently |
| Vague or very short (~20 words) | Student may need more scaffolding for written communication |
| Strong vocabulary from Weeks 1–3 | Terms are being acquired |
| No course vocabulary at all | Terms may be recognised but not yet productive |
| Near-native fluency in a "B1" student | Flag for comparison with oral performance later |

Collect all papers when time is up. File them — you'll reference these at micro-defence scoring.

### Setup (1:00–1:07)

Collect diagnostic papers. Announce triad groups (display list or read aloud). Students move to sit with their group, open their artifact on their screen.

**Optional phone recording:** If you have lanyards, offer them now.

Display:

> If you have a phone and a lanyard, you can record your explanation. This is for you — so you can listen to yourself later and see if you said what you meant. It's not graded.

Explain the format:

> "You're going to explain your work to two partners. Each person gets 4 minutes. Your partners will listen, then give you feedback: one thing you explained clearly, one question they have. Then we rotate. I'll come around and listen to each group."

Display the rotation plan and leave it up:

> **Round 1** (1:07–1:16): Student A presents → B and C give feedback
> **Round 2** (1:16–1:25): Student B presents → A and C give feedback
> **Round 3** (1:25–1:35): Student C presents → A and B give feedback

### Triad Defences (1:07–1:35)

All groups run simultaneously. Three rounds of roughly 9 minutes each (4 min presentation + 5 min for peer feedback and transition).

**Peer feedback frame** (display throughout):

> **After the presenter finishes:**
> - Partner 1: "One thing you explained clearly was _____."
> - Partner 2: "One question I have is _____."
> - Brief discussion: Did AI's description match what the presenter actually did? Was anything unclear?

**Instructor during triads:** Circulate with scoring sheets. For each group:

1. Arrive, listen to the current presenter for 2–3 minutes
2. When the presenter finishes (or during the feedback window), ask one follow-up question
3. Score the checklist and fluency band
4. Move to the next group

With ~7 groups and 28 minutes across 3 rounds, you will not visit every group in every round. Realistically, you will hear ~15 of ~21 presenters, spending 2–3 minutes with each. The remaining students get scored on what you caught in passing. That's fine for a diagnostic baseline — the scoring sheet's "evidence basis" field tracks this. Note which students you sampled briefly; you can follow up individually in Week 5 if needed.

**Follow-up questions** (pick one per presenter based on what you heard):

| If you notice... | Ask... |
|-----------------|--------|
| Student described what they did but not why | "Why did you organise it that way?" or "Why did you choose that file?" |
| Student used a course term correctly | "You said 'commit.' What does that mean?" (checks productive vocabulary) |
| Student said "it didn't work" without details | "What happened exactly? What did the screen say?" |
| Student described AI's output but not their own decision | "What did you decide to do with what AI gave you?" |
| Student's oral level seems much lower than their written work | "Can you tell me in your own words what this Decision Log entry says?" |

**What the peers are doing while the instructor isn't there:** They're still giving feedback using the sentence frames. The two-person audience creates genuine communicative pressure — the presenter has to make themselves understood. The peers' feedback is the quality control: if the explanation was unclear, they'll say so. You don't need to witness every moment for the activity to work.

**Scoring:** Mark during or immediately after each group. Don't deliberate — first impression is fine for a diagnostic baseline.

| What you see | What to do |
|-------------|-----------|
| You heard most of the presentation | Score normally |
| You arrived late and heard only 1–2 min | Score what you heard; note "partial observation" on the sheet |
| You missed a student entirely (rare, but possible) | Ask the student to give you a 2-minute summary after class or at the start of Week 5. Note "deferred" on the sheet. |

**At 1:35, brief whole-class check:**

> "Who explained their work to both partners? Thumbs up." [Most hands.] "Good. You just did the hardest thing in this course: talking about your work without a script. It gets easier."

## Segment C: Peer Response + Goal Document (1:50–2:45)

The instructor is fully available for this segment — no competing micro-defences.

### Structured Peer Response on Goal Documents (1:50–2:20)

**Pairs:** Assign in advance or let students self-pair. Each student should have their partner's Goal Document open via OneDrive sharing. Triad partners from Segment B work well here — pick any two from each group.

Display:

> You've been writing about what you want and what interests you for three weeks. Now: can someone else follow your thinking?
>
> **Step 1 (5 min):** Read your partner's Goal Document — all three entries (Weeks 1–3).
>
> **Step 2 (5 min):** Write answers to these two questions:
>
> 1. What seems to interest your partner most?
> 2. What's unclear, or what would you want to know more about?
>
> **Step 3 (15 min):** Share your answers with your partner. Discuss:
>
> - "Here's what I noticed about your thinking..."
> - "I wasn't sure what you meant when you said _____."
> - "It sounds like you're interested in _____. Is that right?"
>
> Give your written responses to your partner so they have them.

**Collect the written answers** (Step 2) at the end of this activity. This gives you a record of each student's evaluative reading without requiring real-time monitoring.

**What you're listening for** (circulate freely — you're not running defences):

| What you hear | What to say |
|--------------|------------|
| "I think you're interested in _____" | "Good — you identified their main interest from their writing. Did they agree?" |
| "I didn't understand this part" | "Tell them what was confusing. What would help you understand?" |
| "Oh, I changed my mind about that" | "That's fine — what made you change? Can you explain it to your partner?" |
| Silence / "It's good" | "What's one specific thing your partner wrote that you could picture? Start there." |
| Partners finished early | "Read your partner's Step 2 answers about your writing. Do you agree with what they noticed? Is there something they missed?" |

---

### Goal Document: Taking Stock (2:20–2:35)

Display:

> This is your fourth Goal Document entry. Look back at what you've written. Your thinking has probably changed since Week 1.
>
> **Write about where you are now.**

**Prompt** (display):

> What have you learned in 4 weeks? What can you do now that you couldn't before? What interests you most right now? Has your thinking changed since Week 1?

**Sentence frames** (display):

> - "The most interesting thing I've learned is _____."
> - "I want to try _____ next because _____."
> - "My thinking changed about _____ because _____."
> - "I can now _____, which I couldn't do before."

Students write entry 4 (~10 min). Some students' interests will have been consistent; others will have changed direction. Both are good.

> "Changing your mind after trying things is what learning looks like. If you want the same thing you wanted in Week 1, that's fine too — you know more about it now."

This is the first time students practise **evaluative and reflective language**: looking back, taking stock, assessing their own growth. The sentence frames scaffold this. Students who finish early can add a sentence about what they want to try next.

---

### Closing (2:35–2:45)

> "You've been practising the most important skill in this course: saying what you want, testing whether your words work, and revising when they don't. Some of you want the same things you wanted in Week 1. Some of you have completely new ideas. Both are fine. The skill is the same."
>
> "Starting next week, Phase 2: Building. You'll try new kinds of tools and discover more about what you can do."
>
> "I'll read your Goal Documents this week and give you brief feedback."

**If anyone recorded their triad defence:** Remind them to listen to it before next week. "Listen to yourself explaining your work. Did you say what you meant? What would you say differently?"

No AI session review this week — the diagnostic, triad defence, and peer response are the reflection activities.
