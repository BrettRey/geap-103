# GEAP 103 Week 1: Instructor Materials Package

**Title:** What Can a Computer Do for Me?
**Phase:** Discovery (Week 1 of 14)
**Contact time:** 3 × 55 min (165 min total: 150 min instruction + 15 min break)
**Date prepared:** 2026-03-24

This file contains everything you need to prepare slides and run the class for Week 1. The lesson plan (`lesson-plans/week-01.md`) has the pedagogical rationale. This file has the concrete content: what goes on slides, what to say, what to display. In case of conflict, the lesson plan governs.

**Week 1 design note:** Many students miss Week 1. Nothing here is a hard prerequisite for Week 2. Latecomers can catch up using the instructions at the end of this file. The session has two goals: (1) students leave with a working folder structure and a Goal Document, and (2) students believe this course will be worth their time. The Goal Document gets the most time because it's the only extended writing in the week.

---

## Segment A: Hook + Exploration (0:00–1:00)

### Settling In + Course Framing (0:00–0:10)

First day. Students are finding seats, checking they're in the right room, arriving late. Don't fight it.

Instructor introduction (brief, ~2 min). Then display or say:

> This course is about two things: learning to say what you want clearly in English, and discovering what you can do with tools most people don't know how to use.

---

### Showcase (0:10–0:15)

Two examples of things real people built by describing what they wanted to AI. Keep it fast – one minute per example.

**Example 1: Study habits tracker**

- Show: a working app on a phone screen with colourful charts showing study hours by day.
- Say: "This person wanted to see if they were actually studying as much as they thought. They described what they wanted to track and how to display it. They don't write code."

**Example 2: Portfolio website**

- Show: a personal website with an About Me page, a project gallery, and a contact form.
- Say: "This person needed something to send with job applications. They described what sections they wanted and how it should look."

**Key line** (display):

> Both of these were built by people who described what they wanted in English. That's what this course teaches.

"Turn to your partner. Did either of those make you think of something you wish you could do?" Brief pair exchange, 1 min. Move on.

**Note for instructors:** You don't need these exact examples. Find two things that show what's possible – something practical and something creative. If you've built something with AI yourself, show that.

---

### File System Exploration (0:15–0:45)

**Vocabulary reference slide** (display and leave up for the rest of the session):

| Term | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| **folder** | A container for files, like a drawer in a filing cabinet |
| **file path** | The address that tells you where a file lives (e.g., OneDrive > geap-103 > week-01) |
| **file extension** | The letters after the dot (.docx, .xlsx, .pdf) that tell the computer what kind of file it is |
| **cloud sync** | When files are saved on the internet AND your device at the same time |
| **version** | A saved copy of your file at a specific moment – you can go back to an older version |

Do NOT pre-teach these. Students will encounter every one of these concepts in the next 30 minutes. Name them as they come up: "That's called a file path. It tells you where the file lives. Look at the reference slide."

**Single-projector rooms:** Tell students to photograph this slide now. You'll need the projector for other content later. Alternatively, print copies (one per pair).

**Guided walkthrough** (0:15–0:25, 10 min including troubleshooting):

> "Watch me find my Downloads folder. On Windows, I open File Explorer and look in the left panel. On a Mac, I open Finder. On the web, I go to OneDrive and look at the top level. Now you try on your device. Raise your hand if you can't find it."

**Budget 5–8 minutes for troubleshooting.** Half the class may need individual help. This is normal for Week 1. Circulate, resolve, don't rush.

**Exploration tasks** (0:25–0:40, 15 min):

Display:

> 1. Find a file from last semester (or any old file on your device)
> 2. Find your Downloads folder
> 3. Find your OneDrive (the top level)
>
> On paper, sketch a map of where your files actually are. Not where they should be – where they are right now. Boxes for folders, lines connecting them.

Circulate. This is where you name vocabulary:

| What you see | What to say |
|-------------|------------|
| Student finds a file | "What's the *file path* to that? Read it to me. Start from the top." |
| Student sees .docx or .pdf | "That's the *file extension*. It tells you what kind of file it is. What program opens a .docx?" |
| Student sees the OneDrive icon | "See that cloud? That means this file is *synced* – it's on the internet and on your device." |
| Student can't find anything | "Where do you save things? Your phone? Email? Let's start there." |

**Pair comparison** (0:40–0:45, 5 min):

Display:

- "Show your map to your partner. Where are your files? Can you find them?"
- "What's different about your partner's system?"

2–3 pairs share one difference or surprise with the class.

> Most of you found that your files are scattered or hard to find. That's normal. Now let's set up a system that fixes that.

---

### Folder Setup (0:45–1:00)

Display a minimal template:

```
geap-103/
  week-01/
  goal-document/
  decision-logs/
  portfolio/
```

> Create this in your OneDrive now. Just these five folders. You'll add more week folders as we go – or next week, you can ask AI to create them for you.

Students create the structure (10 min). Circulate, help, keep it moving. Common issues: creating folders inside the wrong place, misspelling, not finding OneDrive.

**While students work, name the convention:**

> "Notice the names: lowercase, hyphens instead of spaces. No capitals, no spaces. This matters because some tools can't handle spaces in folder names. Lowercase with hyphens works everywhere."

**If time allows** (students finished folders quickly): "While you're here, go to github.com and create an account. Use your Humber email. You'll need this from Week 3 onward." Students who already have an account can help neighbours. This is optional — catch-up instructions cover it for anyone who doesn't do it today.

Once most students are done, one quick check:

> "What's the file path to your goal-document folder?"

## Segment B: Goal Document (1:15–2:00)

This is the main event. Students produce their first piece of English in the course.

### Launch (1:15–1:22)

Display or say:

> Every week you'll write in a document called your Goal Document. It's where you practise saying what you want, what you're interested in, and how your thinking is changing. You're not committing to anything. You're practising the skill of figuring out what you want and putting it into words.

Students create a new document in their geap-103/goal-document/ folder.

File name (display):

```
goal-document-yourname.docx
```

**Budget 5–7 minutes for this.** Some students will need help finding the folder they just created, naming the file, or opening Word. Circulate.

---

### Writing (1:22–1:42)

**Prompt** (display on slide):

> Think about your life, your studies, or your work. Write 3–5 things you wish your computer could do for you. These don't have to be realistic. Write what you actually want.

**Sentence frames** (display on slide):

- "I wish my computer could _____."
- "It would help me if I could use a computer to _____."
- "I want to learn how to _____."
- "I spend a lot of time on _____, and I think a computer could help because _____."

Students write (20 min). Circulate, encourage, help with language.

| What you see | What to say |
|-------------|------------|
| Student staring at blank screen | "Think about something that takes you a long time. What do you wish was easier?" |
| Student writing very little | "You only need 3 wishes. They can be short. 'I wish my computer could help me cook' is enough." |
| Student writing a lot | "Good. Which one interests you most?" |
| Student writing what they think you want | "I don't want anything specific. Write what YOU actually want. There are no wrong answers." |

---

### Pair Share (1:42–1:55)

> Read your list to your partner. Which wishes do you have in common? Which one interests you most right now?

Each partner explains one wish. Then 2–3 volunteers share one wish with the class.

> You'll come back to this list. Some of these wishes will grow, some will change, some you'll drop completely. That's what learning looks like.

---

### Next Week Preview (1:55–2:00)

> Today you discovered some things people can do with computers, and you started writing about what YOU want. Next week: you'll try telling AI what you want and see what happens. The question will be: can you say what you mean clearly enough for AI to help?

Reminders (display):
- Bring your device next week
- Your geap-103 folder should exist in OneDrive
- Your Goal Document should be in the goal-document folder

## Segment C: AI Session Review (2:00–2:30)

This is the student's first AI interaction. It previews Week 2 and creates a record the student can build on.

### Setup + Student Review (2:00–2:20)

Display:

> Open any AI tool on your device – Copilot, ChatGPT, Claude, or anything you have. On your phone is fine. **Voice mode is encouraged** – talk to AI like you'd talk to a classmate.
>
> **First, paste this as text (then switch to voice):** "Speaking practice. I'm a B1 English student practising explaining my work. Ask me follow-up questions. If my explanation is vague, tell me what's unclear and ask me to be more specific. Don't do the explaining for me."
>
> **Then, your task:** Tell AI what you set up today and what you want to learn in this course. AI will ask you follow-up questions. Try to answer them.
>
> Start with: "Today I set up my course folder and wrote about what I want to learn. I'm interested in _____."
>
> When AI asks a follow-up, answer it. If you get stuck, say: "I'm not sure how to explain that. Can you ask me a different question?"

**Budget the first 5 minutes for access.** Students will be figuring out how to open AI on their device. Some will need to create accounts. Some will use a phone app. Help them get in; don't worry about which tool. **If a student can't get into any AI tool**, they have two options: watch their partner's screen and tell them what to type, or write their review on paper ("Today I learned _____. Next week I want to try _____."). Either way, they produce language.

Once they're in, they have 10 minutes for a short exchange. Spoken or typed – voice is better if available.

> If you can, save your conversation. Copy it into a document in your week-01 folder, or screenshot it on your phone. You'll want to look back at this later.

**Why voice:** This is the first of a weekly speaking practice. Each week, students practise explaining their work to AI, which asks follow-up questions and gives coaching feedback. By Week 4, students will explain their work to real partners in a triad defence. The session review is low-stakes rehearsal for that.

**Pair share** (5 min): "What did AI ask you? Could you answer? What was hard to explain?"

---

### Instructor Reflection (2:20–2:27)

On the projector, ask AI with the class watching:

> "I just taught a first class to B1 English learners. We did file system exploration, folder setup, and a Goal Document where students wrote wishes about what they want their computer to do. What might I have missed? What should I watch for next week?"

Read the response together. Brief reaction: "Does this match what you experienced today?"

**Instructor note:** You don't need to agree with what AI says. If the response is unhelpful or wrong, say so: "That's interesting, but I'm not sure about that one." Students seeing you evaluate AI critically is itself a teaching moment.

This models using AI as a thinking partner – the same skill the course develops.

---

### Buffer (2:27–2:30)

For overflow from any segment that ran long. If nothing ran long, dismiss early. First day of class; students appreciate getting 3 minutes back.

### If You Missed Week 1

Welcome to GEAP 103. Here is what you need to do before Week 2.

**1. Create your course folder structure in OneDrive.**

```
geap-103/
  week-01/
  goal-document/
  decision-logs/
  portfolio/
```

Use lowercase and hyphens. No spaces, no capitals.

**2. Create your Goal Document.**

In the goal-document folder, create a new Word file called `goal-document-yourname.docx`.

Write 3–5 things you wish your computer could do for you. Use these sentence starters:

- "I wish my computer could _____."
- "It would help me if I could use a computer to _____."
- "I want to learn how to _____."
- "I spend a lot of time on _____, and I think a computer could help because _____."

These don't have to be realistic. Write what you actually want.

**3. Read and recognise these 5 words.** You'll encounter them in class; for now, read them so you know them when you hear them.

| Term | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| **folder** | A container for files on your computer, like a drawer in a filing cabinet |
| **file path** | The address that tells you where a file lives (e.g., OneDrive > geap-103 > week-01 > notes.docx) |
| **file extension** | The letters after the dot (.docx, .xlsx, .pdf) that tell the computer what kind of file it is |
| **cloud sync** | When your files are saved on the internet and your computer at the same time |
| **version** | A saved copy of your file at a specific moment – you can go back to an older version |

**4. Create a GitHub account.**

Go to github.com/signup. Use your Humber email. Apply for the [Student Developer Pack](https://education.github.com/pack) (free — gives you GitHub Copilot). Verification may take a few days, so do this now.

**5. Test your AI access before Week 2.**

Open an AI tool on your device – Copilot (copilot.microsoft.com), ChatGPT (chatgpt.com), Claude (claude.ai), or any other. Make sure it works. If you want to try the CLI, [install GitHub Copilot CLI](https://docs.github.com/en/copilot/how-tos/set-up/install-copilot-cli) and run `copilot` once to authenticate. If you have trouble, email the instructor before class.

**6. Come to Week 2 ready.**

Bring your device with OneDrive accessible. Make sure your geap-103 folder exists and your Goal Document is inside it.
