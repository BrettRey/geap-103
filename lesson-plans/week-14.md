# GEAP 103 Week 14 Lesson Plan: Did I Build What I Wanted?

**Context:** 3 x 55 min (150 min instruction + 15-min break), B1 learners, BYOD, Office 365 + Copilot
**Week 14 of 14** | Phase 3: Creation (final week)

**Learning Objectives (Week 14):**
1. Finalise and submit the final Artifact Package (product + process documentation + verification note + sharing evidence) (CLO 6)
2. Explain what you built, what you learned, and how your goals changed, in oral presentations to the class (CLOs 2, 5)
3. Demonstrate role-based speaking in collaborative group presentations (CLO 7)

**No new vocabulary.** The 40 terms from Weeks 1-9 should be part of active vocabulary by now.

**Goal Thread -- Final Reflection (Oral):**
There is no written Goal Document entry this week. The Goal Document prompt ("Is this what I set out to make? What changed?") is answered orally during the sharing. The shift from written to oral is deliberate. The course ends with the student's voice, not their writing.

**Session review this week:** "Explain your final artifact to AI as if AI were a new student who will take this course next semester. What should they know?" AI role: Future student. Communicative demand: Teaching/transferring knowledge to a newcomer. This happens early in the session, before the sharing event.

## Segment A: Final Submissions + Session Review (0:00--0:40)

### 0:00--0:05 | Framing
**Format:** Whole class
**Language skills:** Listening

"This is the last class. One thing is due today: your final Artifact Package. Then we share."

Display submission checklist:

| Submission | What's included |
|-----------|----------------|
| **Final Artifact Package** | The artifact itself + process documentation (own words) + verification note + evidence of sharing |

**Evidence of sharing — concrete examples:** A peer check-in screenshot (Week 9), a session review transcript where you explained the artifact, a PR comment exchange where a partner reviewed your work, a photo of your group presentation. At least one documented instance required.

"If your artifact isn't perfect, that's fine. The documentation matters as much as the product. If your verification note honestly names what you didn't finish, that's better than pretending everything is done."

**Deadline:** Submit by end of Segment A (0:40). Late submissions by 11:59 PM tonight.

### 0:05--0:25 | Finalisation and Submission
**Format:** Individual work with instructor circulation
**Language skills:** Writing (process documentation, verification notes), reading (own portfolio)

Students finalise and submit. Tasks:
1. **Complete the artifact** if needed
2. **Write or revise the verification note:** What was checked, what was found, what was not completed
3. **Ensure process documentation** is in their own words (use the process table from Week 9)
4. **Compile evidence of sharing** (peer check-ins, session review transcripts, collaborative work)

Instructor circulates:
- "Can you read me your verification note? Does it sound like you?"
- "What's in your sharing evidence? The peer check-in from Week 9? The session review transcript?"
- Reassurance: "Submit what you have. Honest documentation is the skill."

### 0:25--0:27 | Bridging Activity
**Format:** Individual, silent

"Before the sharing event, one last conversation with AI. Think about a new student who will take this course next semester. What would you tell them? What do they need to know?"

### 0:27--0:38 | Session Review (Speaking Practice)
**Format:** Individual, AI voice mode
**Language skills:** Speaking (teaching/transferring knowledge, responding to follow-ups)

**Role-setting prompt** (on slide): "Speaking practice. I'm a B1 English student practising explaining my work. Ask me follow-up questions. If my explanation is vague, tell me what's unclear and ask me to be more specific. Don't do the explaining for me."

**This week's scenario** (on slide): "Explain your final artifact to AI as if AI were a new student who will take this course next semester. What should they know about what you built and what you learned?"

AI role: Future student.

Students talk to AI for 10 minutes. The AI asks: "What was the hardest part?" "What would you do differently?" "What advice would you give me?"

This is the final session review. The scenario is reflective and forward-looking — a fitting end to 14 weeks of speaking practice.

**Self-check** (1 min): "Write two sentences: What did the 'future student' ask about? What advice did you give?"

### 0:38--0:40 | Final Submissions
**Format:** Individual

Students who haven't submitted: submit now. Last call.

## Segment B: Sharing Event (0:40--1:35)

### 0:40--0:45 | Sharing Setup
**Format:** Whole class
**Language skills:** Listening

"Now we share. This isn't graded. It's a celebration. But even celebrations benefit from a moment of planning."

"Two rounds: first, individual sharing (you show what you built). Then, group presentations (your group shows what you built together)."

### 0:45--1:10 | Individual Sharing
**Format:** Whole class, informal presentations (2-3 min each)
**Language skills:** Speaking (sustained, personal, evaluative), listening (peers' presentations)

Students share informally. No slides required. Show the artifact on screen, say what you learned, answer one question.

**Structure per student (~2-3 min):**
1. Show what you built (30-60 sec)
2. Say what you learned (30-60 sec)
3. "Is this what you set out to make? What changed?" (30-60 sec)

Instructor and classmates ask one brief follow-up question.

**Pacing — firm limits:** 2 min per student (hard stop). With 20 students: 40 min. For classes over 18: ask for volunteers first (aim for 12-15 presenters), with remaining students sharing in small groups simultaneously during Segment C overflow. For simultaneous sharing: split into two groups in different parts of the room, each with a designated timekeeper student. The instructor moves between groups.

**Tone:** Celebratory. Do not grade. Listen, react, ask genuine questions. "I didn't finish what I planned, but I learned a lot" is a perfect answer.

### 1:10--1:35 | Collaborative Group Presentations
**Format:** Group presentations (3-5 min per group)
**Language skills:** Speaking (role-based), listening (other groups)

Each group presents. Roles are visible:
- **Coordinator** introduces: what they set out to build, how they divided work
- **Builder** demonstrates: shows the artifact, points out key features, describes what AI did vs. what the group did
- **Reviewer** evaluates: what worked, what didn't, what they'd change

**Structure per group (~3-5 min):**
1. Coordinator introduction (1 min)
2. Builder demonstration (1-2 min)
3. Reviewer evaluation (1 min)
4. One question from the class (1 min)

**Firm limit:** 3 min per group (hard stop). With 7 groups: 21 min.

## Segment C: Wrap-Up (1:50--2:45)

### 1:50--2:00 | Overflow Presentations (if needed)

Complete any remaining individual or group presentations.

### 2:00--2:15 | "What Can You Do Now That You Couldn't in Week 1?"
**Format:** Pair discussion, then whole class
**Language skills:** Speaking (evaluative, comparative), listening

"Not about your artifact. Not about your grade. About you. What can you do now that you couldn't do in Week 1?"

**Pair discussion with A/B turns** (5 min): "Tell your partner one specific thing you can do now. Not something big and abstract — something specific."

**Whole-class sharing** (5 min): Volunteers share. Instructor writes responses on the board.

**Instructor note:** Students are producing evaluative, comparative language ("I can now..., which I couldn't...") orally, without sentence frames, in a low-stakes setting. If the course worked, they can do this.

### 2:15--2:25 | Transfer to Degree Programs
**Format:** Pair task, then whole class
**Language skills:** Speaking (matching, explaining), listening

"You're moving into your degree programs. The tools might change. The skills don't."

Display the left column only:

| What you learned here |
|----------------------|
| Telling AI what you want |
| Evaluating whether output is right |
| Writing help requests and documentation |
| Working on shared projects with version control |
| Explaining your work to others |
| Organising files and versions |
| Knowing which AI tool to use for what |
| Checking AI's work with a second opinion |
| Starting fresh when a conversation gets confused |
| Telling AI who to be (persona) |

**Pair task with A/B turns** (5 min): "With your partner, match each skill to a situation in your degree program or future job. Where will you use this? Be specific — not 'everywhere' but 'in my business course when...'"

**Whole class** (5 min): Volunteers share matches. Instructor reveals the right column for any that weren't mentioned:

| Where it shows up next |
|----------------------|
| Every course that allows AI tools |
| Research, assignments, professional work |
| Group projects, IT support, workplace |
| Collaborative assignments, co-op |
| Presentations, interviews, meetings |
| Every course, every job |
| Choosing the right tool for each task at work or school |
| Verifying any output before relying on it |
| Recognising when to reset instead of pushing through |
| Framing requests for any audience -- human or AI |

"The one thing I hope you take from this course is that English is the control layer. If you can say what you mean clearly, you can get a computer to help you do almost anything."

### 2:25--2:35 | Course Evaluation
**Format:** Individual
**Language skills:** Writing (evaluative)

Students complete the institutional course evaluation. Instructor steps away while students complete it.

### 2:35--2:45 | Closing
**Format:** Whole class

"You started this course 14 weeks ago. Some of you didn't know what a file path was. Some of you had never used AI. All of you have now built something real, documented how you built it, shared it with others, and explained it in English."

"You also learned something most people never learn: how to manage AI, not just use it. You learned to check AI's work, to compare tools, to start fresh when conversations decay, to tell AI who to be, and to know when not to use AI at all. Those skills transfer to every tool you'll ever use."

"Your Goal Documents tell the story of how your thinking changed -- 13 entries, from 'I wish my computer could...' to 'When I started I thought... now I think...' I've enjoyed reading them."

"Good luck in your degree programs. You're ready."

- **Confirm Git Episodes graded** (submitted Week 12, graded by now).
- **Confirm oral reflections scored** (Week 13, should be done).
- **Return final Goal Document comments.** Brief comments on the full 13-entry arc.
- **Calculate final grades.** Components: Decision Logs 30% + Oral Micro-Defences 25% + Artifact Packages 25% (7% + 8% + 10%) + Git Episodes 10% + Oral Reflection 10%.
- **Flag any students whose process documentation language is markedly different from their micro-defence oral level** for brief oral follow-up (AF v6 authenticity guidance).

## Language Development Summary

- **Vocabulary:** No new terms. All 40 terms deployed naturally in presentations, sharing, and discussions. This week is the best evidence of acquisition: students use terms without prompting across multiple genres.
- **Speaking:** **Session review** (10 min AI voice: explaining to a future student — the final and most forward-looking scenario); individual informal presentations (2-3 min sustained); collaborative group presentations (role-based: Coordinator introduces, Builder demonstrates, Reviewer evaluates); pair discussion of growth; whole-class sharing
- **Reading:** Own portfolio (final review); submission checklist; transfer table; course evaluation
- **Writing:** Final Artifact Package components (process documentation, verification note); session review self-check (2 sentences); course evaluation
- **Listening:** Peers' individual and group presentations; instructor's transfer discussion; whole-class sharing; closing remarks

## Assessment Alignment

- **Final Artifact Package due (10% of final grade):** Product + process documentation + verification note + evidence of sharing. Most complex artifact package. (CLOs 1, 4, 6)
- **Session review** (formative, not graded): Final scenario — explaining to a future student. The last of 14 weeks of speaking practice.
- **Informal presentations:** Not graded. Evidence of oral fluency development. Instructor may note observations for B1.2 exit evidence.
- **Goal Document:** No written entry. The oral sharing answers the Goal Document prompt. Instructor returns final comments on the 13-entry arc.
- **Course complete.** Final grades: Decision Logs 30% + Oral Micro-Defences 25% + Artifact Packages 25% (7% + 8% + 10%) + Git Episodes 10% + Oral Reflection 10%.
