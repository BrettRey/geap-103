# GEAP 103 Week 13 Lesson Plan: Making It Better

**Context:** 3 x 55 min (150 min instruction + 15-min break), B1 learners, BYOD, Office 365 + Copilot
**Week 13 of 14** | Phase 3: Creation

**Learning Objectives (Week 13):**
1. Evaluate the strength of portfolio evidence against specific CLOs, distinguishing strong evidence from weak (CLO 4)
2. Draft an Evidence-Indexed Reflection with claim sentences, evidence links, and honest evaluation of evidence quality (all CLOs)
3. Write a final Goal Document entry that traces change across the full semester (evaluative, temporal comparison)
4. Complete and submit Decision Logs (8 entries, 30%) with calibrated self-assessments (CLOs 3, 4)

**No new vocabulary.** Consolidation of all 40 terms from Weeks 1-9. Students should be using course terminology fluently in their reflections and peer feedback.

**Goal Thread -- Evaluation:**
This is the final written Goal Document entry. Students read their entire Goal Document from the beginning and write about how their goals changed and what they learned about what they want. The language demands are the highest of the semester: temporal comparison across 13 weeks ("When I started, I thought... Now I think..."), evaluative judgment ("The most important thing I learned is... because..."), and honest self-assessment of growth. This entry is where the Goal Document arc resolves: the student who wrote "I wish my computer could..." in Week 1 now writes about what they can do and how their thinking changed.

## Segment A: Self-Assessment Calibration (0:00--0:40)

### 0:00--0:10 | Decision Log Self-Assessment Patterns
**Format:** Whole class, instructor-led
**Language skills:** Listening, reading (instructor feedback)

Instructor returns observations about Decision Log self-assessment patterns across the class. This is not individual feedback on specific entries (that happened in Rounds 1 and 2). This is a whole-class discussion of calibration:

"Over the semester, I've noticed patterns in how you rate your own work. Some of you consistently rate yourselves lower than I would. Some of you rate yourselves higher. Some of you are very close to how I'd rate you. None of these is wrong, but I want you to think about why."

Display anonymised examples (3-4, with student permission or composited):
- A self-rated "Excellent" that the instructor rated "Proficient" -- why? (e.g., the goal was clear but not ambitious; the entry was solid but routine)
- A self-rated "Developing" that the instructor rated "Proficient" -- why? (e.g., the student was too hard on themselves; the entry showed genuine critical evaluation that the student didn't recognise as strong)
- A well-calibrated self-assessment -- what makes it accurate? (the student's justification sentence matches the rubric criteria)

"Your self-assessment on the Decision Log isn't just a box to check. The justification sentence is where you practise evaluative judgment. 'I rated this Proficient because I stated my goal clearly and documented what I changed, but I didn't try a different approach' is useful. 'I rated this Excellent because it's good' is not."

### 0:10--0:35 | Worked Example: Full Evidence-Indexed Reflection
**Format:** Instructor demonstration with think-aloud
**Language skills:** Listening, reading (portfolio evidence, reflection template)

"In Week 14, you'll submit an Evidence-Indexed Reflection. It's a structured document where you make a claim about each of the 7 CLOs, link to evidence, and evaluate how strong that evidence is. Today I'm going to show you what the full process looks like, not just the template."

Instructor demonstrates the reflection for two CLOs using a sample portfolio. The demonstration shows what "evaluating evidence strength" actually looks like:

**CLO 4 (Evaluate AI text):**
- Claim: "I can evaluate AI outputs and make justified decisions about what to keep, change, or reject."
- Evidence: Link to Decision Log entry 5 (where the student caught Copilot generating an incorrect formula).
- Evaluation: "This DL entry is strong evidence for CLO 4 because it shows I noticed a specific error that Copilot made -- the SUM formula included a header row -- and I explained why it was wrong and what I changed. I didn't just say 'I fixed it.' I said what the error was and how I caught it."
- Growth area: "I'm better at catching factual errors than at evaluating whether AI output matches my intention. My early DL entries show I accepted output that was technically correct but didn't do what I wanted."

**CLO 1 (Manage files and versions):**
- Claim: "I can manage files and versions using appropriate workflows."
- Evidence: Link to Week 4 file-system artifact.
- Evaluation: "This is weak evidence by itself because anyone can organise a folder. The screenshot shows folders with names, but it doesn't show WHY I organised it this way or whether I can find things quickly. A stronger piece of evidence would be my Week 9 commit history, which shows I consistently used descriptive file names and folder paths across 7 weeks of project work."
- Growth area: "I still sometimes save files to my desktop instead of my project folder. I need to build the habit of filing things immediately."

"Notice two things. First: I'm honest about which evidence is strong and which is weak. The file-system screenshot is real evidence, but it's not convincing on its own. I said that. Second: the growth areas are specific. Not 'I need to improve' but 'I still save files to my desktop.' That specificity is what makes this useful."

### 0:35--0:40 | Reflection Template and Expectations
**Format:** Whole class, Q&A
**Language skills:** Listening, reading (template)

Display the Evidence-Indexed Reflection format:

For each of the 7 CLOs:
- 1 claim sentence ("I can now...")
- Links to 1-2 portfolio artifacts as evidence
- 1 sentence evaluating the strength of that evidence
- 1-2 sentences on what the student would still need to work on

Total: ~600-800 words (~75-100 words per CLO).

"This is due next week. Today you'll start drafting it. You don't need to finish, but you should have at least 3-4 CLOs drafted by the end of today, with honest evidence evaluation. The rest you finish before Week 14."

## Segment B: Portfolio Curation + Project Refinement (0:40--1:35)

### 0:40--0:50 | Pre-Task Planning
**Format:** Individual reflection
**Language skills:** Reading (own portfolio, own Goal Document), writing (evidence mapping)

"Before you start writing your reflection, map your evidence. Open your portfolio. Open your Goal Document. Open your Decision Logs. For each CLO, write down where your best evidence is. Not your only evidence -- your BEST evidence."

Students create a simple evidence map (can be a table, a list, or annotations in their reflection document):

| CLO | Best evidence | Where to find it | Strong or weak? |
|-----|--------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| 1 | DL entry 6 (complex goal) | Decision Log | Strong -- shows goal revision |
| 2 | Restore episode | Git Episodes folder | Medium -- shows the steps but not my understanding |
| ... | ... | ... | ... |

This planning step prevents students from writing generic claims without evidence. If they can't find evidence for a CLO, that's important information for the reflection.

### 0:50--1:10 | Reflection Drafting + Cross-Group Peer Review
**Format:** Individual writing (15 min), then pair work with partners from different groups (5 min)
**Language skills:** Writing (evaluative, metacognitive), reading (partner's draft), speaking (feedback)

Students draft their Evidence-Indexed Reflection, starting with the CLOs where they have the strongest evidence. Write at least 3-4 CLO entries.

After 15 minutes of writing, students pair with a partner from a DIFFERENT collaborative group. Each partner reads the other's draft reflection for 1-2 CLOs.

**Peer review question** (displayed): "Is this evidence convincing? If I looked at the artifact they linked to, would I agree that it shows what they claim? What's missing?"

Partners give verbal feedback (2-3 min each). The point is not to edit the reflection but to test whether the evidence-to-claim link is convincing to someone who didn't produce the work.

### 1:10--1:25 | Collaborative Artifact: Final Push
**Format:** Group work (assigned roles continue)
**Language skills:** Speaking (negotiation, task coordination), writing (artifact production, PR descriptions)

"Your collaborative artifact is due at the end of today. Use this time to finish. Coordinators: check your Issues. What's done? What's left? Builders: what needs to be completed? Reviewers: what still needs checking?"

Groups work on their collaborative artifact. Roles continue as assigned in Week 11:
- **Coordinator:** Checks Issues, confirms what's complete, identifies remaining tasks
- **Builder:** Completes remaining production work
- **Reviewer:** Reviews final PRs, checks accessibility, writes or updates verification notes

### 1:25--1:35 | Collaborative Artifact Submission + Contribution Statements
**Format:** Individual + group
**Language skills:** Writing (descriptive, accountable)

Groups submit their collaborative artifact (commit to shared repository, or submit through established process).

Each group member then writes an individual **contribution statement** (2-3 sentences):
- What they built or produced
- What PRs they submitted
- What they reviewed

**Displayed guidance:** "Write 2-3 sentences describing your contribution. Be specific: 'I wrote the introduction and the data section. I submitted 2 PRs and reviewed 3. I also ran the Accessibility Checker on the final version.' This is checked against your Git history. It's not graded separately, but I use it to verify that everyone contributed."

This is an accountability mechanism, not a graded assessment. The instructor checks contribution statements against Git history. If a student claims contributions not visible in the history, or if the history shows contributions not claimed, that warrants a conversation.

## Segment C: Goal Document Final Entry + Decision Logs Due (1:50--2:45)

### 1:50--1:55 | Framing the Final Goal Document Entry
**Format:** Whole class, instructor-led
**Language skills:** Listening

"You've been writing in your Goal Document for 13 weeks. This is your last written entry. Next week the Goal Document becomes oral -- you'll share informally, not write. So this is your chance to put into words what this semester meant."

"Here's what I want you to do: go back to the beginning. Read your Week 1 entry. Read every entry after that. Notice how your language changed. Notice how your thinking changed. Then write about what you see."

"This is hard. It requires you to hold your past self and your present self in the same sentence. 'When I started, I thought...' -- that means remembering what you actually thought, not what you think you should have thought. 'Now I think...' -- that means being honest about where you are, not where you wish you were."

### 1:55--2:20 | Goal Document: Final Written Entry
**Format:** Individual reading (10 min), then individual writing (15 min)
**Language skills:** Reading (own Goal Document, all 12 previous entries), writing (evaluative, temporal comparison, subordination)

**Step 1 -- Read (10 min).** Students read their entire Goal Document from Week 1 to Week 12. No writing yet. Just reading.

Instructor circulates quietly. Some students will be surprised by how different their early entries sound. Some will notice patterns they didn't see week to week. Both reactions are valuable.

**Step 2 -- Write (15 min).**

**Prompt** (displayed): "Read your Goal Document from the beginning. Write a final entry that answers: What did I learn about what I want? How did my goals change? What can I do now that I couldn't do in Week 1?"

**Sentence frames** (displayed):
- "When I started, I thought _____. Now I think _____."
- "The most important thing I learned is _____ because _____."
- "My goals changed from _____ to _____ because _____."
- "I can now _____, which I couldn't do before because _____."

Students write their final Goal Document entry. This entry requires the most complex language of the semester: temporal comparison across 13 weeks, subordination ("Although I planned to..., what actually happened is..."), evaluative judgment ("The most important thing..."), and causal reasoning spanning multiple weeks of experience.

**Instructor note:** This is the culminating language moment of the Goal Document arc. The student who wrote "I wish my computer could help me with my homework" in Week 1 is now writing sentences like "My goals changed from wanting the computer to do things for me to understanding that I need to tell the computer what I want clearly, because I learned that the computer does exactly what you say, not what you mean." That transformation in articulation IS the course.

### 2:20--2:25 | Commit Goal Document
**Format:** Individual
**Language skills:** Writing (commit messages)

Students commit their final Goal Document entry. "Final written Goal Document entry: reflection on the full semester."

### 2:25--2:30 | Instructor Reads Goal Documents
**Format:** Announcement
**Language skills:** Listening

"I'll read your Goal Documents this week -- all 13 entries -- and return brief final comments. This is the third and last time I read them. I'll comment on the arc: how your thinking and your language developed from Week 1 to now."

### 2:30--2:35 | Decision Log Final Check
**Format:** Individual
**Language skills:** Reading (own Decision Logs), writing (self-assessment)

"Your 8 Decision Log entries are due by the end of class today. Take 5 minutes right now to check: Do you have 8 entries? Does each one have a self-assessment with a justification sentence? Is there at least one entry where you documented rejecting or substantially revising AI output?"

Students review their Decision Logs and make any final adjustments to self-assessment ratings or justification sentences. This is not a time to write new entries from scratch -- if a student has fewer than 8, they should have been writing entries throughout the semester. But brief revisions to self-assessment calibration are appropriate.

### 2:35--2:40 | Decision Log Submission
**Format:** Individual
**Language skills:** Writing (commit messages)

Students commit and submit their completed Decision Logs (8 entries). Commit message should describe the submission: "Decision Logs complete: 8 entries with self-assessments."

### 2:40--2:45 | Closing
**Format:** Whole class

- "You wrote your final Goal Document entry today. If you read from Week 1 to now, you can see your own language changing. That change is real."
- "You also submitted your Decision Logs. Eight entries documenting how you worked with AI, what you decided, and why. That's a real record of your judgment developing over 13 weeks."
- "Your collaborative artifact is submitted. Next week you'll present it, and your roles will be visible: the Coordinator introduces, the Builder demonstrates, the Reviewer evaluates."
- "Next week is the last class. You'll submit your final Artifact Package, Git Episodes, and Evidence-Indexed Reflection. You'll also share your work informally. The tone is: look what you can do. Come ready to show what you built and say what you learned."

## After Class

- **Read all Goal Documents (entries 1-13)** and return brief final comments by Week 14. Comment on the arc: how the student's thinking and language developed across the semester. This is the third and final reading (after Weeks 4 and 8). Focus on: Is the progression visible? Has the student's ability to articulate what they want grown? Is the final entry genuinely evaluative, or is it generic?
- **Grade Decision Logs** (8 entries, 30%). Use the rubric from Assessment Framework v4. Pay attention to: ambition progression across entries, self-assessment calibration, explore/exploit balance, presence of failure/rejection episodes.
- **Check contribution statements against Git history.** Flag any significant mismatches for conversation with the student before Week 14.
- **Review collaborative artifacts.** Note quality and completeness. Groups that didn't finish should be given a brief extension or opportunity to submit by the start of Week 14.
- **Spot-check Evidence-Indexed Reflection drafts** (if submitted early). Students should have at least 3-4 CLOs drafted. Flag students who haven't started for a check-in at the start of Week 14.

## Language Development Summary

- **Vocabulary:** No new terms. All 40 terms from Weeks 1-9 in active use across reflections, peer feedback, contribution statements, and project work. Students should be deploying course terminology fluently in evaluative and metacognitive writing.
- **Speaking:** Cross-group peer feedback on reflection drafts (evaluating evidence quality); collaborative project coordination (role-based task negotiation); brief verbal exchanges during contribution statement verification
- **Reading:** Own Decision Logs (all 8 entries for final review); own Goal Document (all 12 previous entries -- the most sustained reading task of the semester); partner's reflection draft; instructor's self-assessment calibration examples; Evidence-Indexed Reflection template
- **Writing:** Evidence mapping (analytical, organisational); Evidence-Indexed Reflection draft (3-4 CLOs: claims, evidence links, evidence evaluation, growth areas -- the most linguistically demanding writing task of the semester); contribution statement (descriptive, accountable); Goal Document final written entry (temporal comparison, subordination, evaluative judgment); Decision Log self-assessment revisions; commit messages
- **Listening:** Instructor's self-assessment calibration discussion; worked example of full Evidence-Indexed Reflection (including evidence strength evaluation); partner's verbal feedback on reflection; closing announcements about Week 14

## Assessment Alignment

- **Decision Logs due (8 entries, 30% of final grade):** Final submission. Self-assessments with justification sentences. At least one failure/rejection episode expected. Entries 6-8 assessed summatively (feedback was provided on entries 1-2 in Round 1 and entries 3-5 in Round 2).
- **Collaborative artifact due:** Submitted by end of session. Contribution statements from each group member, checked against Git history. Not graded separately, but used to verify meaningful contribution (relevant to CLO 7 and the collaborative component of the final Artifact Package).
- **Evidence-Indexed Reflection:** Drafting begins. Due Week 14 (10% of final grade). Students should have 3-4 CLOs drafted by end of class.
- **Goal Document:** Final written entry (Week 13 -- entry 13). Not graded. Instructor reads after this week and returns brief final comments on the full semester arc. This is the third and final Goal Document reading point.
- **CLOs:** All CLOs are active this week. The Evidence-Indexed Reflection explicitly requires students to map evidence to all 7 CLOs. The collaborative artifact completion addresses CLO 7. The Decision Log submission addresses CLOs 3 and 4. The Goal Document entry addresses the language development that underlies all CLOs.
