# GEAP 103 Week 10 Lesson Plan: Building My Thing (Part 2) + Submission

**Context:** 3 x 55 min (150 min instruction + 15-min break), B1 learners, BYOD, Office 365 + Copilot
**Week 10 of 14** | Phase 2: Building (final week)

**Learning Objectives (Week 10):**
1. Complete and submit a mid-semester artifact package (product + process documentation + sharing evidence) (CLO 6)
2. Write a verification note describing what was checked and how (CLOs 4, 5)
3. Compare the finished artifact with the original plan and write honestly about the gap (evaluative, subordinating language)
4. Begin collaborative project formation by identifying shared interests and complementary skills (CLO 7 begins in earnest)

**No new vocabulary.** Consolidation of all 40 terms from Weeks 1-9.

**Goal Thread -- Reflection on Gap:**
This is a major language moment. Students compare what they planned with what they actually built. The language requires subordination ("Although I planned to..., what I actually..."), concession ("Even though I wanted..., I ended up..."), and honest evaluation ("The biggest difference is... because..."). This is the transition from Phase 2 (planning and justifying) to Phase 3 (evaluating and comparing). The session review this week has students present their artifact to AI as a client, which primes the written reflection.

## Segment A: Verification and Quality Check (0:00--0:40)

### 0:00--0:05 | Framing
**Format:** Whole class
**Language skills:** Listening

"Today you finish and submit your mid-semester artifact package. The package is three things: what you built, documentation of how you built it in your own words, and evidence of sharing. But before you submit, you check your work. Not just 'does it look OK?' but 'did I build what I said I would?'"

### 0:05--0:25 | Worked Example: The Gap Between Plan and Result
**Format:** Instructor demonstration
**Language skills:** Listening, reading (Goal Document text, Accessibility Checker output)

Instructor demonstrates the full final review process using their own (or a sample) artifact:

**Step 1 -- Re-read the Goal Document.** Display a Goal Document entry (the project plan) and the finished artifact side by side. "Here's what I said I would build. Here's what I actually built. Let's compare."

**Step 2 -- Name the gap honestly.** Think aloud: "I planned to include three charts, but I only finished two. The third was too complicated -- I couldn't get Copilot to generate the right format, and I ran out of time. That's not a failure. That's useful information. The honest thing to write is: 'I planned three charts but completed two because the third required a chart type I couldn't figure out in the time available.'"

**Step 3 -- Run the Accessibility Checker.** Run the checker on the artifact. Fix one issue on screen (e.g., missing alt text). "This is part of verification. You check that what you built is usable, not just finished."

**Step 4 -- Write the verification note.** Write on the projector:

> "I checked my spreadsheet with the Accessibility Checker. It found one image without alt text, which I added. I also tested the formulas by entering sample data and comparing the results with a calculator. The budget totals match. I re-read my Goal Document from Week 8 and confirmed that I built the budget tracker I described, except for the savings projection chart, which I did not complete."

"This is in my own words. It says what I checked, what I found, and what I didn't finish. If this sounds very different from how I talk, that's a red flag."

### 0:25--0:40 | Artifact Package Requirements Review
**Format:** Instructor-led, Q&A
**Language skills:** Listening, reading (requirements)

Display the artifact package components:

| Component | What to submit | Notes |
|-----------|---------------|-------|
| **Product** | The artifact itself | Must be functional. Accessibility features where applicable. |
| **Process documentation** | Written in your own words: what AI did, what you changed and why, what you checked | Not AI-generated prose. Use your process table from Week 9. |
| **Verification note** | What you checked, what you found, what you fixed, what you didn't finish | Honest. Includes the gap between plan and result. |
| **Evidence of sharing** | How you explained or showed your work to someone else | Week 9 peer check-in counts. Any other sharing you did. |

"Questions? **Deadline: submit by end of Segment B (1:35). If you need more time, submit by 11:59 PM tonight.** If your artifact isn't perfect, that's fine. The documentation matters as much as the product."

**Evidence of sharing -- minimum threshold:** At least one documented instance of explaining or showing your work to someone else. The Week 9 peer check-in counts. Students who missed Week 9 can satisfy this by saving their session review transcript (AI-as-client conversation) or by explaining their artifact to a partner during collaborative formation today.

## Segment B: Planning + Finalisation + Submission (0:40--1:35)

### 0:40--0:45 | Pre-Task Planning
**Format:** Individual reflection
**Language skills:** Reading (own Goal Document), writing (priority list)

"Open your Goal Document. Re-read your project plan from Weeks 7-8 and your task list from Week 9. Check your process documentation table. What's done? What's not? What do you need to finish in the next 35 minutes?"

Students write a brief priority list:
- What is complete
- What still needs to be done
- What they will skip if they run out of time

### 0:45--1:20 | Finalisation and Submission
**Format:** Individual work with instructor circulation
**Language skills:** Writing (process documentation, verification note), reading (AI output, Accessibility Checker results)

Students work on three tasks in whatever order makes sense:

1. **Finish the artifact.** Complete any remaining work on the product.
2. **Write process documentation.** In their own words: what AI did, what they changed, what they checked. Students who used the process table from Week 9 build on it. Students who didn't: reconstruct from memory and their Decision Log entries.
3. **Write the verification note.** Run the Accessibility Checker (if applicable). Compare the artifact against the Goal Document plan. Write what was checked, what was found, and what was not completed.

Instructor circulates:
- **Submission support:** Help students compile their package (where to put files, how to submit).
- **Process documentation check:** "Can you read me your process documentation? Does it sound like how you'd explain this to me in person?" If it sounds markedly unlike the student's spoken English, prompt them to rewrite in simpler language.
- **Gap honesty check:** "What didn't you finish? Write that down. Naming what you didn't do is part of the documentation."

### 1:20--1:35 | Commit and Submit
**Format:** Individual
**Language skills:** Writing (commit messages)

Students commit their complete artifact package to their portfolio repository. Then submit through the established submission process (OneDrive folder, LMS upload, or GitHub link).

Commit message: "Submitted mid-semester artifact package: [brief description of artifact] + process documentation + verification note."

Students who finish early: begin reading their full Goal Document from Week 1 (preparation for the reflection in Segment C).

## Segment C: Session Review + Reflection + Collaborative Formation (1:50--2:45)

### 1:50--1:52 | Bridging Activity
**Format:** Individual, silent

"You just submitted your artifact. In two minutes, you'll present it to AI as if AI were a client who paid for the work. Think about: what does it do? Who is it for? What's one decision you made during the process?"

### 1:52--2:04 | Session Review (Speaking Practice)
**Format:** Individual, AI voice mode
**Language skills:** Speaking (presenting work to a non-technical audience, responding to follow-ups)

**Role-setting prompt** (on slide): "Speaking practice. I'm a B1 English student practising explaining my work. Ask me follow-up questions. If my explanation is vague, tell me what's unclear and ask me to be more specific. Don't do the explaining for me."

**This week's scenario** (on slide): "Present your mid-semester artifact to AI as if it were a client. Explain what it does, who it's for, and one decision you made during the process."

AI role: Client who commissioned the work.

Students talk to AI for 10-12 minutes. The AI asks follow-up questions as a client would: "Why did you choose this approach?" "What would you change if you had more time?" "Who exactly is this for?"

This is the first scenario where the audience is non-technical. Previous weeks practised explaining to peers, teammates, and help desks. A client audience requires simplifying technical details and foregrounding purpose over process.

**Self-check** (1 min): "Write two sentences: What did the 'client' want to know most? What was hardest to explain?"

**Note:** Voice mode starts a new chat. The prompt is said aloud or pasted at the start.

### 2:04--2:16 | Goal Document: Reflection on the Gap
**Format:** Individual writing
**Language skills:** Writing (evaluative, subordinating, concessive)

"You just presented your artifact to a client. Now write about the gap between what you planned and what you built. What you said to AI can help you — you've just practised articulating it."

**Prompt** (displayed): "Compare what you planned with what you built. Write 3-4 sentences about the gap."

**Sentence frames** *(optional -- use if you're stuck)*:
- "I set out to build _____. What I actually built is _____."
- "The biggest difference is _____ because _____."
- "If I had more time, I would change _____."
- "I learned that _____ is harder/easier than I expected because _____."

**Writing** (12 min): Students write entry 10 in their Goal Document. The session review primes this: students have just articulated the gap orally and can now write a more considered version.

**Instructor note:** This is a Phase 3 language moment. Students are moving from "I will..." to "Although I planned..., what I actually..." This requires subordination, hedging, and concession -- features that B1 learners are still developing. The sentence frames scaffold this, but monitor whether stronger students are moving beyond them.

### 2:16--2:40 | Collaborative Project Formation
**Format:** Whole class, then small groups with structured turn-taking
**Language skills:** Speaking (expressing preferences, negotiating), listening (partner's ideas)

"You've built something on your own. For the last four weeks, you'll build something with a partner or small group. This is a new thing, not a continuation of your individual project."

**Step 1 -- Interest inventory** (3 min): Each student writes on a card or shared document:
- "I'm interested in _____."
- "I'm good at _____."
- "I want to learn _____."

**Step 2 -- Gallery walk** (8 min, default for classes of 15+): Cards posted on wall or shared slide. Students circulate, read all cards, and note 2-3 people whose interests or skills complement theirs. *(For small classes under 15, a round-robin works: each student shares aloud, 30 seconds each.)*

**Step 3 -- Group formation** (13 min): Instructor facilitates pairing/grouping based on shared interests or complementary skills. Groups of 2-3. Students do not need to share the same project type; complementary skills are valuable.

Once groups form, they begin discussing: "What do *we* want to build together?" This is the beginning, not a commitment.

*DL feedback round 2 is returned at the start of Week 11, not today. Students have zero bandwidth for reading evaluative feedback after submitting, presenting, reflecting, and forming groups. Returning it fresh at Week 11 opening means students can actually process it before writing entries 6-8.*

### 2:40--2:45 | Closing
**Format:** Whole class

- "You submitted your mid-semester artifact package today. That's a real thing you built, documented, and shared."
- "You also wrote about the gap between plan and result. That writing skill -- comparing intention with result -- is what the rest of the course is about."
- "Starting next week: Phase 3. You'll work with your group to build something together. The question shifts from 'What do I want?' to 'What do we want?'"
- "If you saved your session review transcript (the AI client conversation), you can add it to your artifact package as supplementary sharing evidence. Submit it the same way you submitted the rest."
- "I'll return your Decision Log feedback at the start of next week so you can read it with fresh eyes."

## After Class

- **Grade mid-semester artifact packages.** Use the Artifact Package rubric (Assessment Framework v6). Focus on: does the process documentation sound like the student? Is the verification note honest? Is there evidence of sharing? Return feedback by Week 12.
- **Note students who did not submit** or submitted incomplete packages. Follow up at the start of Week 11.
- **Review collaborative group formations.** Identify any students who were absent or didn't find a group. Plan to integrate them in Week 11.
- **Decision Log feedback round 2:** Prepare for return at start of Week 11 (not today -- students need fresh bandwidth). Include confirmation signal ("target addressed" / "target still developing") for each entry.
- **Session review patterns:** Were students able to present to a non-technical audience? Note students who struggled with the "client" framing -- this skill feeds the artifact sharing event at Week 14.

## Language Development Summary

- **Vocabulary:** No new terms. Consolidation of all 40 terms from Weeks 1-9 as students use relevant vocabulary in artifact work, process documentation, verification notes, and session review.
- **Speaking:** **Session review** (12 min AI voice: presenting artifact to a non-technical client -- the first scenario with a non-technical audience, requiring simplification and purpose-foregrounding); collaborative project formation (expressing interests, negotiating with potential partners)
- **Reading:** Own Goal Document (full re-read for reflection); instructor's verification note example; Accessibility Checker output; DL feedback round 2 with confirmation signal; partner's interest inventory cards
- **Writing:** Verification note (descriptive, evaluative); process documentation (own words); Goal Document reflection entry 10 (subordination, concession, evaluation -- the most linguistically demanding GD entry so far); interest inventory (brief self-description); commit messages; session review self-check (2 sentences)
- **Listening:** Worked example of gap analysis and verification; AI follow-up questions as client; partner's interests during group formation

## Assessment Alignment

- **Mid-Semester Artifact Package due (8% of final grade):** Product + process documentation + verification note + evidence of sharing (CLOs 1, 4, 6)
- **Decision Log feedback round 2:** Prepared this week, returned at start of Week 11. Written feedback on entries 3-5 with confirmation signal ("target addressed" / "target still developing"). Entries 6-8 are assessed summatively.
- **Goal Document:** Reflection on gap (entry 10). Not graded. Instructor reads at Week 13 (next reading point).
- **Session review** (formative, not graded): Week 10 scenario practises presenting to a non-technical audience -- builds toward the Week 14 artifact sharing event
- **CLO 7** (Collaborate): Collaborative project formation begins. Groups formed. No assessment yet, but the collaborative artifact and Git Episodes (collaboration episode) will be assessed in Weeks 11-14.
- **Phase transition:** Last week of Phase 2 (Building). Next week begins Phase 3 (Creation). Goal Document complexity shifts from "Planning and justifying" to "Evaluating and comparing."
